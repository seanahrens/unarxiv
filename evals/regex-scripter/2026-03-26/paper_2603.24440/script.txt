Abstract.

Computer-use agents (CUAs) hold great promise for automating complex desktop workflows, yet progress toward general-purpose agents is bottlenecked by the scarcity of continuous, high-quality human demonstration videos. Recent work emphasizes that continuous video, not sparse screenshots, is the critical missing ingredient for scaling these agents. However, the largest existing open dataset, ScaleCUA, contains only 2 million screenshots, equating to less than 20 hours of video. To address this bottleneck, we introduce , a large-scale ecosystem of expert video demonstrations and dense annotations for professional desktop computer-use agents. At its core is , which provides approximately 10,000 human-demonstrated tasks across 87 diverse applications with continuous 30,fps screen recordings, kinematic cursor traces, and multi-layered reasoning annotations averaging 497 words per step, totaling approximately 55 hours and 6 million frames of expert video, more than 2.5 times the largest existing open dataset. Unlike sparse datasets that capture only final click coordinates, these continuous video streams preserve the full temporal dynamics of human interaction, forming a superset of information that can be losslessly transformed into the formats required by existing agent frameworks. further provides two complementary resources: , a rigorous benchmark for evaluating grounding and planning capabilities in CUAs, and , a large-scale grounding dataset with 56K annotated screenshots and over 3.6 million UI element annotations. Together, these resources provide dense, causal supervision in which every element on screen is labeled and every action is logged. Preliminary evaluation reveals that current foundation action models struggle substantially with professional desktop applications ( approximately equals 60 percent task failure rate). Beyond benchmarking, 's rich multimodal corpus supports emerging research directions including generalist screen parsing, continuous spatial control, video-based reward modeling, and visual world models. All data and models are publicly released.

Introduction.

The vision of intelligent agents that can operate alongside humans at a computer, understand our goals, navigate our interfaces, and execute complex workflows on our behalf, has long captured the imagination of researchers and practitioners alike. Computer-use agents (CUAs) promise to achieve this vision and transform how we work: from automating repetitive data entry to orchestrating sophisticated 3D modeling pipelines, from streamlining scientific analyses to managing the ever-growing complexity of our day-to-day digital lives. In an era where digital literacy is a bottleneck and interface complexity grows exponentially, these agents offer a compelling vision: computers that transition from passive tools to active collaborators.

However, realizing this vision has proven difficult. Despite significant advances in vision-language models and foundation agents, today's CUAs remain surprisingly brittle. They excel at simple web tasks but falter when using professional desktop applications, such as 3D modeling software, IDEs, and specialized tools that underpin modern knowledge work. This problem is exacerbated for popular but non-mainstream applications, such as open-source software, where models struggle to navigate unfamiliar interfaces.

For CUAs to be truly useful, they must take a user's task, formulate a plan, and ground that plan in executable actions. The fundamental challenge is the lack of high-quality training data that encompasses rich, dense annotations for both planning and grounding. Recent works have attempted to fill this gap with automatically curated or synthesized datasets, but these often suffer from noise inherent to automated generation. When human-curated datasets do exist, they typically cover only partial aspects of the problem, such as spatial grounding without temporal context. Moreover, even comprehensive human datasets such as OpenCUA rely on action discretization, resulting in sparse screenshots that omit intermediate visual feedback between actions. Concurrent work has independently confirmed this limitation, arguing that screenshot-based agents are fundamentally unable to process high-framerate video, perform long-horizon tasks, or scale to competent agents. For instance, ScaleCUA, the largest existing open dataset, contains 2 million screenshots, which equates to less than 20 hours of video at 30,fps. Such sparse data lacks the temporal continuity required to build visual world models or learn the continuous spatial control policies necessary for human-like cursor movement. Bridging this gap requires richly annotated human data providing dense, multi-faceted feedback: continuous video trajectories, kinematic action traces, and precise UI grounding. Together, these signals enable models to capture the full spectrum of computer-use intelligence.

Curation of .

We introduce , a large-scale ecosystem of continuous expert video demonstrations and dense UI annotations for professional desktop applications. Where previous efforts often rely on synthetic accessibility trees for Desktop environments , recaption existing annotations or focus exclusively on web browsers , our approach centers on recording high-fidelity human behavior as continuous 30,fps video. We prioritize professional-grade applications and dense, manual annotation to create a dataset for training agents on real-world workflows. This unified data engine underlies the ecosystem, supporting three complementary datasets: for training agents on complex workflow execution through continuous video trajectories, for training agents on UI grounding, and for benchmarking visual perception and planning. Below, we describe our data collection pipeline, from application selection to dense annotation collection.

To support general-purpose computer-use agents, we selected 87 open-source applications across 12 categories. These applications range from software development (VS Code) and content creation (Blender, Inkscape, Krita) to finance and productivity (GnuCash, LibreOffice). By focusing on open-source applications with permissive licenses, we ensure the dataset can be freely released while encompassing a wide range of domains. These applications mirror the functionality of popular closed-source software (for example, LibreOffice vs. Microsoft Office), making the dataset broadly applicable. Further details are provided .

Instead of procedurally generating goals or using templates, we asked human experts to design tasks they would perform in a real work setting. The tasks range from simple actions (for example, renaming a folder, creating a document) to complex workflows (for example, editing a spreadsheet, running a simulation, applying subtitles to a video). We ensure that each task is well-defined and comprehensive. This approach ensures the collected trajectories represent coherent, goal-oriented behavior rather than random exploration. In total, annotators completed over 10,000 task demonstrations across 87 applications.

Annotators executed these tasks while our system captured continuous screen video at 30 frames per second, producing approximately 55 hours and 6 million frames of uncut expert demonstration footage across all tasks. Alongside the video stream, we logged every mouse click, drag, scroll, and keystroke with millisecond precision, yielding synchronized kinematic cursor traces. By preserving the complete visual state at every frame, the dataset encodes the full temporal dynamics of expert desktop interaction, including the intermediate cursor movements and visual feedback between actions that sparse screenshot-based datasets discard.

From this continuous visual stream, we extract specific keyframes, that is, snapshots of the interface immediately preceding state-changing user actions (for example, clicks or text entry) to serve as the basis for grounding. This selection ensures that annotations correspond to the user's decision-making context. Annotators then manually label every visible UI element in these keyframes with bounding boxes. For each element, they provide a textual label. This label is the element's name when available, the displayed text for shorter strings, or a concise summary in the case of long passages such as source code or detailed descriptions. We also extract OCR using PaddleOCR to extract raw text specifically for these longer segments. Additionally, approximately 50 percent of elements are classified into one of eight high-level functional categories , adding a layer of semantic structure to the geometric ground truth.

This robust data engine serves as the single source of truth for the entire . The collected data is methodically processed to construct three complementary resources: for complex agentic execution through continuous video trajectories, for fine-grained UI grounding, and for visual perception and planning evaluation. By grounding these complementary resources in a shared foundation of expert human behavior, provides a holistic platform for diagnosing and advancing the capabilities of computer-use agents. We envision this rich, multimodal corpus will serve as a catalyst for future research, supporting tasks beyond our current scope and enabling the community to build the next generation of generalist computer-use agents.

 is a desktop-centric benchmark for evaluating the visual perception and planning capabilities of computer-use agents. It comprises 450 high-quality task demonstrations originating from the spanning diverse applications and interaction patterns, and serves as the primary evaluation benchmark within the ecosystem.

The benchmark is designed to specifically test three fundamental agentic capabilities. Element Grounding evaluates the agent's ability to precisely localize UI elements given a textual query (for example, "Click the Save button"), assessing the foundational visual understanding required to translate semantic intent into screen coordinates. Layout Grounding tests the agent's comprehension of the interface structure by requiring it to identify and group functionally related elements (for example, "Select the navigation bar"), going beyond individual element recognition to evaluate holistic scene understanding. Finally, Action Prediction assesses the agent's planning capability by providing a high-level goal and the current screen state, and asking it to predict the next correct action (for example, click, drag, type), connecting visual perception to executable decision-making. By leveraging the dense annotations and expert trajectories from , provides a multi-faceted diagnosis of where agents fail, that is, whether in seeing the interface, understanding its structure, or planning the next move. Previous evaluations on identified visual grounding as the primary bottleneck limiting agent performance . Consequently, the analysis here focuses on re-evaluating the grounding capabilities of state-of-the-art multimodal models to assess recent progress and persistent challenges. (A detailed analysis of the Action Prediction task is provided in ). The primary observation from is that overall performance has nearly doubled in the year since the introduction of ; the previous state-of-the-art, UI-TARS-72B, has been significantly outperformed by newer architectures, with MAI-UI-32B achieving a new high of 47.7 percent in average accuracy. Despite this rapid progress, substantial scope for improvement remains. Breaking down the results by task type reveals that while models are excelling in the Basic and Functional categories, with top models approaching 60 percent accuracy, the Spatial split remains stubbornly difficult across the board, indicating that reasoning about spatial relationships on the screen is still a major hurdle. This could be attributed to a lack of such training data in existing computer-use datasets. The results also demonstrate that scaling up model parameters yields consistent and predictable benefits for grounding. For instance, the OpenCUA family shows a clear upward trajectory, improving by 7.6 absolute points as capacity increases from 7B to 72B parameters. This scaling is likely due to improvements in reasoning capabilities, which help distinguish dense, visually similar UI regions. This is further evident in PhiGround-7B, where pairing the model with an o3 planner improves its average performance by an impressive 9.0 absolute points (from 27.2 percent to 36.2 percent), demonstrating that providing models with reasoned instructions mitigates execution errors.

Multi-layered Reasoning Annotations.

A key challenge in leveraging video demonstration data for training action models is the semantic gap between raw interaction logs and the structured reasoning required by modern vision-language agents. Raw recordings capture what the user did (for example, "click at coordinates (x, y) "), but not why they did it or what visual elements they were targeting. To bridge this gap, we enrich each video with dense trajectory annotations that provide action-level intent descriptions for effective instruction following.

We address this by adopting the Multi-layered reasoning trajectory synthesis pipeline from OpenCUA that transforms our annotated recordings into richly described state-action sequences. For each keyframe s sub t in a task trajectory, we employ Claude-Sonnet-4.5 to generate four complementary annotation layers: (1) an observation providing detailed description of the current screen state, identifying relevant UI elements and their spatial arrangement; (2) a thought chain explaining the reasoning process connecting the high-level task goal to the immediate action choice; (3) an action description specifying the intended action in natural language grounded to visual elements rather than raw coordinates; and (4) a reflection analyzing the outcome and enabling self-correction signals for training. This synthesis process converts each trajectory step into the format tau sub t equals (s sub t, o sub t, r sub t, d sub t, a sub t, s sub t plus 1, ref sub t) , where o sub t is the observation, r sub t is the reasoning chain, d sub t is the action description, a sub t is the executable pyautogui code, and ref sub t is the reflection. This rich structure enables training on both action prediction (predicting a sub t from s sub t and d sub t ) and reasoning generation (producing r sub t given s sub t and the task instruction).

Our synthesized trajectories average 496.7 words per step (157.4 observation, 194.3 reasoning, 17.7 action description, 127.4 reflection), providing the dense supervision signal that recent work identifies as critical for training capable GUI agents .

Action Prediction Evaluation.

To understand how well current foundation action models generalize to complex desktop applications our can offer, we conduct a preliminary evaluation on 256 sampled tasks spanning 87 applications. Rather than serving as a definitive benchmark, this evaluation is designed to reveal the landscape of model capabilities and limitations across diverse desktop domains.

Model Performance.

We evaluate OpenCUA-7B and OpenCUA-32B , the two publicly available foundation action models at different scales. presents the results. Both models exhibit limited accuracy under task-level prediction. Across 256 sampled tasks (up to 1,999 predictions), the 32B model achieves 37.7 percent @50px, while the 7B model reaches only 16.5 percent. Scaling from 7B to 32B yields consistent improvement: +21.2 percentage points at @50px, with mean pixel distance decreasing from 387.5 to 274.2. However, absolute performance remains modest. Per-application performance varies widely (from 3.6 percent to 73.3 percent @50px) with specialized creative tools and canvas-based applications posing the greatest challenge, while web-like interfaces achieve higher accuracy (detailed analysis in Appendix , error distance distribution in Appendix ).

Human Evaluation of Predicted Trajectories.

The automated pixel distance metrics cannot capture whether a prediction constitutes a semantically correct action, as a prediction 30 pixels from the ground truth may still click the correct button, while one 10 pixels away may land on an adjacent element. To complement the distance-based evaluation, we conduct a human evaluation that directly assesses the functional correctness of the 32B model's predicted actions across all action types.

Implications for Foundation Action Models.

Both automated and human evaluations converge on the same conclusion: current foundation action models struggle substantially with professional desktop applications, achieving only 37.7 percent @50px and 57.6 percent human-verified stepwise accuracy. The wide per-application variance confirms that the core difficulty lies in the diverse visual vocabularies and interaction patterns of professional desktop software, where existing training data is scarce. directly targets this domain gap through domain coverage of 87 professional applications where models struggle most, video scale of approximately 55 hours of continuous 30,fps recordings that capture the full temporal dynamics of expert interaction, annotation density of approximately 497 words per step providing rich supervisory signal for action planning, and action diversity spanning complex interaction primitives (drags, fine-grained mouse control) that web-centric datasets underrepresent.

Universality and Application Potential of .

A primary design principle of is universality: by capturing continuous video streams, kinematic cursor trajectories, and expert-verified dense annotations, our data constitutes a superset of the information required by current paradigms. Concretely, can be losslessly transformed into the specialized formats of every leading agent framework (such as screenshot-action pairs, state-action-next-state triplets, or continuous kinematic traces) without discarding any recorded signal. This generality means that as new training paradigms emerge, remains directly applicable without re-collection. We highlight four application frontiers below, with detailed descriptions in Appendix . (1) Generalist Screen Parsing. Current MLLMs struggle to reliably parse desktop screenshots into structured UI elements . provides dense, human-verified bounding-box annotations covering all interactable elements, including canvas-based and custom-drawn widgets missed by DOM-based approaches, together with functional descriptions that enable semantics-aware element captioning. Recent works, such as ScreenParse , have already explored this possibility on a subset of data in GroundCUA. With denser, richer annotations , we believe it can enable even more powerful and robust models for screen parsing. (2) Continuous Spatial Control. GUI-Cursor argues that discrete coordinate prediction is insufficient for high-precision tasks, proposing continuous cursor movements from visual feedback instead. captures intermediate cursor movements and complete video context, preserving the kinematic priors (for example, Fitts's Law deceleration) needed to train imitation learning or offline RL policies for feedback-driven navigation. (3) Visual World Models. Training GUI world models such as those based on textual planning , visual generation or code rendering requires dense (s sub t, a sub t, s sub t plus 1) triplets that most sparse-screenshot datasets cannot provide. Our 30fps video recordings, paired with timestamped actions, provide exactly these causal pairs, enabling action-conditioned video generation and visual lookahead planning for desktop workflows. (4) Video-Based Reward Modeling. Evaluating whether an agent has successfully completed a task remains a key bottleneck for scaling CUA training with reinforcement learning. ExeVRM demonstrates that video-based reward models, which assess task completion from execution video rather than agent internals, can outperform proprietary models. 's continuous expert video recordings with task-level annotations provide an ideal source of positive demonstrations for training such reward models, while the dense step-level annotations enable fine-grained, step-wise reward signals beyond binary task-level labels.

Conclusion.

We presented , a comprehensive ecosystem for training and evaluating desktop computer-use agents. At its core, provides the largest open expert video corpus for desktop computer use, comprising approximately 55 hours of continuous 30,fps recordings across 10,000 tasks and 87 professional applications, with kinematic cursor traces and multi-layered reasoning annotations. This is complemented by pixel-precise grounding and rigorous evaluation within a single coherent ecosystem. Our evaluations reveal that spatial grounding remains the primary bottleneck for current foundation action models in desktop environments, with performance varying widely across application domains. Beyond its immediate use as a benchmark and training corpus, 's continuous video streams, kinematic traces, and dense annotations support emerging paradigms such as generalist screen parsing, continuous spatial control, and visual world models. We will release all data, benchmarks, and models to the community, and hope will serve as a foundation for building the next generation of general-purpose computer-use agents.
