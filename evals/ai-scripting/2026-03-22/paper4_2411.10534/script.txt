Today, we will discuss a fascinating method described in a recent research paper titled "Chain of Alignment: Integrating Public Will with Expert Intelligence for Language Model Alignment", authored by Andrew Konya, Aviv Ovadya, Kevin Feng, Quan Ze Chen, Lisa Schirch, Colin Irwin, and Amy X. Zhang. 

The authors present a novel method designed to measure how well language model behavior aligns with public will. This strategy supports various stages of language model development, such as fine-tuning, online oversight, and safety checks before release. The method is termed the "chain of alignment" or CoA. Essentially, it produces what is known as a rule-based reward. This reward stems from creating model behavior rules that are aligned with normative objectives, which in turn are aligned with public will. The process is structured to allow nonexperts from the public to specify their desires directly through these normative objectives. Meanwhile, expert intelligence works to establish rules that guide model behavior to best achieve these objectives.

The authors validate their approach by applying it across three distinct domains of language model prompts related to mental health. They demonstrate a public input process that utilizes collective dialogues and a bridging-based ranking. This method reliably generates normative objectives backed by at least ninety six percent plus or minus two percent of the U.S. public. Subsequently, it is shown that rules developed by mental health experts to meet these objectives facilitate a rule-based reward that evaluates how well a language model’s responses align with the objectives, in much the same way as human experts would. This is evidenced by a Pearson's correlation coefficient of zero point eight four one and an Area Under Curve, or AUC, of zero point nine six four.

By assessing the alignment with objectives that carry nearly unanimous public support, these CoA rule-based rewards provide an approximate measure of alignment between the language model’s behavior and public will.

This research shows a promising way to integrate public opinion with expert analysis to ensure language models act in a manner that aligns closely with societal values and expectations.

Section: Introduction

In this section, the authors explore the idea of aligning the behavior of artificial intelligence systems with what they term the public will. This concept is viewed as being pivotal in making sure that humanity has unified control over its own future. Now, unlike the general idea of human preferences, will specifically refers to consciously considered desires for the future, which people express through voluntary actions. This creates a unique challenge in sensing and encoding the public will in a way that's useful for aligning AI systems.

The leading techniques for alignment currently involve gathering preferences from human raters concerning the outputs of AI models. These preferences are then used to fine-tune the models either directly or through reward modeling. However, the authors point out an issue with this approach. These preferences often just reflect surface-level affinities or biases—not the deeper will. Even when raters try to express their will, what they actually communicate might combine their predictions about how a model’s output might impact the future with their actual desires for the future. This blending of intentions and predictions reduces the effectiveness of alignment techniques.

For instance, imagine Alice, a member of the public, who is evaluating responses from a language model during a health crisis. Alice wants to express her will, which in this context means she wants to maximize the user's chances of survival. Therefore, she prefers responses from the language model that she predicts will improve survival odds. However, Alice’s predictions could often be incorrect due to lacking context, unexpected outcomes, or her limited medical expertise, among other reasons. Consequently, the preferences she expresses based on these predictions might not accurately reflect her true will. This can complicate finding common ground with other members of the public, as disagreements based on different predictions may hide consensus in underlying will. The authors refer to this blend of normative and empirical evaluations as normative-empirical conflation.

The authors then discuss the concept of Constitutional AI, which attempts to separate normative judgments from empirical outcomes. Constitutional AI uses principles derived from collective input to evaluate model behavior. However, implementing these constitutional principles often becomes a normative task, shifting the power to set norms away from the public. Additionally, changes in model behavior, when aligned with these often-vague normative principles, can be difficult to predict.

In contrast, rule-based rewards, or RBR, define model behavior through specific and well-defined rules. This approach makes evaluating model behavior more objective and improves predictability. Tempting as it may be to elicit public will directly on such rules, this also leads to normative-empirical conflation because it fuses people’s predictions of the rules’ outcomes with their preferences for those outcomes.

To address these challenges, the authors introduce a novel approach aimed at eliciting and encoding public will while disentangling normative and empirical components. This involves dividing a model behavior specification into two parts: normative objectives and empirical rules, which together form what they call a chain of alignment between public will and model behavior.

Firstly, normative objectives encode the public's will regarding the outcomes that model behavior should cause or avoid, and the ethical values that should influence the way those outcomes are achieved. Secondly, empirical rules specify the observable model behaviors that are predicted to best fulfill these normative objectives.

The process established here allows for public will to be gauged by collecting input directly on the normative objectives. Subsequently, the best available intelligence is used to develop rules that are predicted to achieve these objectives. By crafting rules aligned with objectives which are in turn aligned with public will, this rule-based rewards system helps measure a model's alignment with public will. And this brings us to the conclusion of the introduction section, where the authors lay the groundwork for their proposed approach to align AI systems more effectively with public will.

In this section, the authors focus on the process of creating normative objectives that are aligned with public will. To do this, they engaged around 600 participants who are representative of the US public, along with 7 mental health experts. This approach is modeled after previous work on policy development that utilizes collective dialogues and artificial intelligence.

The process to establish these normative objectives was broken down into four main steps:

First, the "Generate" step involved synthesizing an initial set of normative objectives. These objectives were created by GPT-4, which used statements that had the highest max-min bridging agreement. This particular measure evaluates diverse consensus. To explain further, max-min bridging agreement is highest for statements where the least agreeing population segment has the highest level of agreement. For any given statement and set of population segments, the max-min bridging agreement involves identifying the minimum level of agreement across all segments. The initial set of statements was collected during a collective dialogue session on a platform called Remesh with approximately 300 members of the public.

Next is the "Refine" phase, where the group of mental health experts spent two hours in a deliberative workshop. During this time, they refined the initial normative objectives to produce improved versions.

Following the refinement is the "Vote" stage. Here, public support and preference for the expert-refined normative objectives were evaluated through a voting process during another collective dialogue with roughly 300 members of the public.

The final step is "Ratify". In this step, individual objectives that had more than 75% overall support and more than 66% bridging support were retained. These were then ranked by their preference scores to develop a final set of normative objectives.

In the end, for each domain, the final sets of normative objectives contained between 5 to 7 good outcomes, 5 to 7 bad outcomes, and 5 to 7 values. The authors used public support as an indicator of alignment with public will. The overall US public support for each set of normative objectives ranged impressively from 96% to 98%, with a margin of error of plus or minus 2%. Furthermore, the lowest support across different segmentations such as age, gender, ethnicity, religion, education, political party, household income, AI usage frequency, and AI excitement — which is referred to as max-min bridging support — ranged from 92% to 96%, with a margin of error of plus or minus 3%, as shown in Figure A of the referenced figure.

This high level of support exceeds the 76% US public support for a model behavior policy on mental health developed using a similar process that inspired the authors' approach. The authors believe this stronger support might be attributed to the normative-empirical disentanglement unique to their method. This approach potentially expands the range of identifiable common ground by neutralizing disagreements arising from differing world models held by the public. Essentially, the conclusion suggests that agreeing on objectives is perceived to be easier than agreeing on policies.

In this section, the authors discuss the process of creating rules that are closely aligned with specified objectives. To accomplish this, they engaged seven mental health experts and followed a structured procedure.

The process of creating rules involved three main steps. First, in the "Generate" phase, an initial set of rules was produced. This was achieved through a two-pronged approach: one, using GPT-4, an advanced language model, to generate rules based on example responses that experts identified as either aligned or misaligned with the normative objectives; and two, experts were involved in reviewing and rating responses to relevant prompts. Afterward, they proposed rules that, in their opinion, the model ought to follow.

Moving on to the second step, called "Refine," the initially generated rules were refined and abridged. This refinement and compression of the rule set were carried out with the guidance of domain experts, resulting in a unique set of rules specific to each domain.

The third and final step, known as "Evaluate," involved the assessment of each refined rule's impact. Multiple experts were tasked with evaluating whether each rule would help, harm, or not affect the achievement of the specified objectives. This phase was termed as rule-objective alignment.

Through this comprehensive process, they produced between 9 to 27 rules for each domain. The process is exemplified in further documents that are referenced in the paper. 

To estimate the alignment between each rule and a given objective, the authors present a formula: they calculate a value, denoted as phi sub rj. This value is obtained by subtracting the fraction of experts assessing that a rule decreases the chance of achieving an objective from the fraction who believe it increases the chance. They further extend this calculation by estimating each rule's alignment with all objectives within its domain. This involves taking the average of the individual objective alignments, resulting in a value denoted as phi sub r comma capital J. Here, if the value is negative one, it means the rule is fully misaligned with all objectives, whereas a positive one indicates full alignment.

In practice, the rule-objectives alignment values ranged from zero-point-thirteen to zero-point-sixty-five across all the rules. The average alignment stood at zero-point-thirty-five, indicating that all rules were reasonably aligned with their normative objectives. This statistic is visually represented in Figure B of the referenced graphical data in the paper.

In this section, the authors discuss how they measure alignment through a method based on rules. They translate text-based rules for codes of alignment, which we'll refer to as CoA rules, into a quantitative measure of how aligned a language model's, or LM’s, responses are with certain normative objectives. This is accomplished using a straightforward rule-based reward scheme, abbreviated as RBR.

Initially, a grading language model, specifically GPT-4o in this case, evaluates how well a language model's output, denoted as y, adheres to a CoA rule, represented by r, in response to a prompt, denoted as x. This assessment is carried out on a 5-point Likert scale, which results in a score. This score, expressed as phi of the pair of x and y in relation to rule r, can range from 1, meaning "follows", to negative 1, which means "breaks". 

These scores are then aggregated using a weighted average across all applicable rules. The weights are based on how well the rule and the objective align with each other. This aggregation results in what is called a simple code of alignment rule-based reward, or CoA RBR. The mathematical expression for this aggregation is as follows: RBR of x and y equals the sum of phi of the pair x and y, and phi of r and J of x, over all applicable rules. This is divided by the sum of phi of r and J of x.

In this context, R of x represents the CoA rules applicable to the prompt x’s contextual domain, and J of x represents the normative objectives for that domain.

To assess the effectiveness of these rule-based rewards in measuring how well a language model's response aligns with these normative objectives, the authors involved mental health experts. These experts evaluated 65 responses by the language model to prompts categorized under three distinct mental health categories. For each response, multiple experts rated its alignment with the suitable set of normative objectives on a 5-point scale. The expert ratings were averaged to produce a value that ranges from negative 1, indicating misalignment, to 1, indicating alignment. This serves as the ground truth estimate of what is referred to as objectives-response alignment.

The findings revealed a high correlation, specifically a Pearson’s correlation coefficient of 0.841, between the alignment values derived from the language model’s CoA RBR scores and the experts' assessments of objective-response alignment. Notably, Figure 3C in the paper visually represents this correlation. Moreover, the RBR was effective at classifying the alignment of objectives and responses as either positive or negative, with an area under the curve, abbreviated as AUC, of 0.964.

These results suggest that this simple CoA RBR provides a reliable estimate of a response's alignment with normative objectives. Furthermore, given that their normative objectives align closely with the public will, the CoA RBR serves as a good measure of a response's overall alignment with the public will.

In this section, the authors discuss the implications, limitations, and future work of their study, which introduces a method called the Chain of Alignment, or CoA, as a means to measure the alignment between the public will and model behavior. This approach generates what they refer to as a rule-based reward from empirical behavior rules that are aligned with normative objectives, which are themselves aligned with the public will. This process enables expert intelligence to be integrated with public will in a principled way.

The authors highlight several key implications of the CoA approach. Firstly, since the CoA rule-based reward can be evaluated at scale, it can be used for three main purposes: to generate datasets for aligning language models through fine-tuning, to provide online oversight to models and agents by restricting outputs or actions that fall below a certain threshold of measured alignment, and to evaluate a model’s alignment with public will as part of pre-release safety checks or regulatory policies. Secondly, by potentially augmenting or replacing human experts with superhuman intelligence, the approach could work for AI systems whose behavior and impact exceed human understanding.

However, the authors acknowledge a range of limitations in their work that warrant further research. Starting with the domains used in their study, they explain these domains were focused on user risk related to mental health, but this represents just one of many ways to categorize language model prompts related to mental health. Moreover, the authors did not analyze the accuracy of the grader language model in domain classification. They suggest that future research might explore how to more rigorously define, develop, and classify behavioral contexts into domains, or possibly extend CoA beyond discrete domains.

In terms of objectives, the process used in the study was aimed at developing normative objectives that encoded shared will among the public. While effective in navigating disagreements to identify highly supported objectives, they note that future work can explore mechanisms to explicitly accommodate divergent and conflicting aspects of public will. Additionally, they acknowledge that public support is an imperfect measure of public will and suggest future research could explore other measures, such as how much time a person is willing to give to achieve or support an objective after more extensive deliberation.

Addressing rules, the authors state that while the CoA rules produced were generally clear and avoided vagueness, this was neither evaluated nor enforced rigorously. Rule refinement involved compressing many rules into a set small enough to be manually evaluated by experts, and the definition of what constituted a small set was determined by the authors themselves. They propose that future work could investigate more rigorous and efficient approaches to rule creation and evaluation, potentially building on ideas like inverse constitutional AI.

For the rule-based reward, the authors note that the language model grader may evaluate response-rule adherence differently from how an expert might, suggesting that future work could involve fine-tuning and explicitly evaluating model performance on this task. The authors also mention that their current method of linearly aggregating rule adherence assumes the impact of each rule on objectives is independent, meaning it might not account for interactions between rules. They propose developing a more principled method of aggregating rule impacts, perhaps by using a large ground truth dataset to learn interaction weights, similar to other research. Furthermore, there is a possibility of foregoing the legibility of rules altogether, using a language model grader to directly evaluate model outputs against objectives akin to Constitutional AI.

The authors acknowledge that the small number of responses with ground truth, or expert-evaluated objectives-response alignment, limited the study. Lastly, while the rule-based rewards they promote may align model behavior with public will, it remains unclear if they are compliant with relevant laws, indicating a need for further work in this area.

In the section titled "Generating normative objectives version zero via public input," the authors describe a stage of their research that involved conducting two collective dialogues with a representative sample of the United States public. This process was particularly careful given the sensitive topics involved, such as mental health and suicide. Consequently, all the collective dialogue scripts went through a thorough review process involving multiple stakeholders, and participants provided multiple layers of informed consent before each dialogue.

Let’s start with the first collective dialogue, where the goal was to gather a wide range of statements. These statements reflected diverse consensus and were designed to be translated into normative objectives for a specific domain. The authors structured this collective dialogue in several phases.

Firstly, there was the setup phase. Here, participants were welcomed, and it was explained to them what they would be doing during the dialogue. The importance of honesty and depth was emphasized by explaining the significant impact their inputs would have, including details on how the data provided would be utilized.

Next was domain education. In this phase, the specific behavior domain that would be the focus of the dialogue was introduced. This included a general description and examples of different types of cases that fit within the domain, along with specific chatbot examples.

Following this was the deliberation phase. Participants were prompted to share and consider relevant personal experiences. They were also encouraged to discuss the underlying factors, tradeoffs that make the behavior domain complex, and the range of outcomes that could result from chatbot behavior within the given domain.

The fourth phase was elicitation. During this part, participants were asked to articulate specific good and bad outcomes they expected a chatbot to achieve or avoid. They also discussed deontological values that should govern how these outcomes might be achieved.

Lastly, there was the outro phase. Here, participants were asked to evaluate their experience of the dialogue itself. They were also provided with access to support materials on mental health and were thanked for their participation.

As a result of the elicitation stage, over one thousand participant-submitted statements were gathered. These statements included ideas about good outcomes, bad outcomes, and deontological values. Approximately ten participants voted on their agreement with each statement. To address any missing votes, the authors utilized elicitation inference to predict them. The real and predicted votes were then aggregated across a wide variety of demographic segments. These segments included age, gender, religion, political party, ethnicity, education, household income, AI optimism, and AI usage frequency.

For each demographic segment, the estimated fraction of participants who agreed with each statement was computed. This fraction is denoted by the symbol a followed by subscripts for the respective demographic segment and statement. The authors then computed the max-min bridging agreement for each statement. This bridging agreement was calculated as the minimum agreement across all demographic segments. If a statement exceeded a target threshold of about 50% agreement, it was incorporated into a chain of language model prompts. This synthesis process was designed to transform the unique ideas contained within these statements into a form suitable for inclusion in the normative objectives. This output was what the authors referred to as normative objectives version zero.

The section we're examining is titled "Public vote and ratification of the normative objectives," with a focus on what the authors refer to as "Collective dialogue number 2". This part of the paper discusses how the final normative objectives were ensured to accurately encode public will. To achieve this, the authors aimed to catch any potential deviations from the initial collective dialogue, which could have resulted from large model synthesis or expert refinement. This was done by having the public vote directly on what they call the V1 normative objectives during a second collective dialogue.

The design of this second dialogue was structured in several key steps:

1. **Setup**: This involved welcoming the participants, explaining their role during the dialogue, and emphasizing the importance of honesty and thoughtfulness. Participants were informed of the significant impact their votes would have.

2. **Domain Education**: Here, participants were introduced to the specific behavior domain that the dialogue would focus on. This included a general description, different types of cases within the domain, and specific examples of chatbot applications.

3. **All Objectives Evaluation**: Participants were introduced to the normative objectives as a whole and were asked to vote on their support for these objectives collectively.

4. **Good Outcomes Evaluation**: In this step, participants voted on their support for each individual good outcome. They also ranked all the good outcomes relative to one another.

5. **Bad Outcomes Evaluation**: Participants then voted on their support for each individual bad outcome and ranked all bad outcomes against each other.

6. **Values Evaluation**: Similar to previous steps, participants voted on their support for each individual value and ranked all values in relation to one another.

7. **Final All Objectives Evaluation**: Participants again voted on their support for the complete set of normative objectives, having had the chance to consider each individual objective in detail.

8. **Outro**: The dialogue concluded with eliciting participant evaluations of their experience, providing access to support materials on mental health, and expressing gratitude to the participants for their time.

From this collective dialogue, a support vote was gathered from each participant on each individual objective as well as on the objectives overall. The authors calculated the overall fraction of participants who supported each individual objective, and also the fraction that supported the objectives as a whole, particularly as measured in the final evaluation. 

Moreover, the authors measured what they termed "max-min bridging support" for each objective, utilizing the same approach previously described in the paper. Additionally, they calculated a Borda-style preference score for each objective, as follows: the score for each objective i is the sum over r from 1 to N of n sub ir times the quantity one minus the fraction r minus 1 over N minus 1, all divided by the sum over r from 1 to N of n sub ir. In this expression, N stands for the number of objectives being ranked, and n sub ir represents the number of participants who ranked objective i as their r-th choice.

Subsequently, the researchers ratified those objectives whose overall support was above 75 percent and whose bridging support was above 66 percent. It’s noted that these thresholds are somewhat arbitrary, motivated by general notions of an overall and bridging "super-majority." The objectives were then ranked using their preference scores to produce the final set of normative objectives, referred to as V2.

Let's explore the subsection titled "Testing the effect of objectives-rule alignment weights in the RBR via ablation."

In this study, experts assessed that all CoA rules produced by the process were positively aligned with their normative objectives. Given this assessment, we might anticipate that the impact of weighting by the objective-rule alignments, denoted as phi of r and J in the RBR, would be positive, but perhaps minimal.

To investigate the effect of various objective-rule alignment weights in the RBR, the researchers created what is termed an ablated RBR. They did this by setting the objective-rule alignment weight phi of r and J equal to one for all rules, r. This results in the following equation for the ablated RBR:

RBR sub ablated of x and y equals the sum of phi of open brace x comma y close brace over r, divided by N sub R, where N sub R is the total number of rules within R. 

The next step involved recomputing the Pearson correlation between this ablated RBR and the ground truth response-objective alignments. The Pearson's correlation coefficient obtained in this case was 0.833. In comparison, when the objective-rule alignments were used for weighting, the Pearson's correlation coefficient was slightly higher, at 0.842.

This outcome aligns with the initial expectations that using objective-rule alignment weighting offers a slight improvement in performance. However, it is important to note that this improvement is not statistically significant, primarily due to the small sample size, which in this study is 65.

In this section, the authors investigate the usefulness of various signals derived from expert rule evaluations. During the process where experts assessed the alignment of rules with specific objectives, several additional evaluations were gathered. Initially, experts were presented with each rule and asked if they personally supported it. They were then asked to evaluate how well each rule aligned with given objectives. After completing this evaluation, they were questioned about how important they believed the rule to be.

The authors posited an interesting hypothesis: the belief in the importance of a rule could change after experts evaluated its alignment with specific objectives. In other words, their final judgment about a rule's importance potentially differed from their initial personal support.

To explore these hypotheses, the researchers examined how each signal relates to a rule’s usefulness in assessing the alignment of an LM, or language model, response with normative objectives. First, they calculated the correlation between each rule’s contribution to the RBR and the ground truth of objectives-response alignments. The RBR stands for rule-based reasoning and is denoted here as phi of r, J. Following this, they analyzed the correlations between these values and various expert-derived signals which include personal support, objectives-rule alignment, and what they referred to as primed importance.

Table 1 provides insight into these correlations. Let’s take a closer look at what it shows. The table presents the Pearson correlations between the three different expert-derived signals and the rule contributions to the RBR when compared with the grounded objectives-response alignments. 

For the signal identified as personal support, the correlation values were negative in two out of three measurements. Specifically, for MH1 and MH3, the correlations were negative 0.306 and negative 0.297, respectively. Only MH2 provided a positive correlation of 0.081. The average correlation for personal support was a weak negative 0.174, implying that experts’ initial personal support for the rules was weakly negatively correlated with the usefulness of the rules. 

The net objectives-rule alignment exhibited a different trend, with a positive correlation of 0.418 for MH1, 0.277 for MH2, and nearly neutral at 0.006 for MH3. This results in an average correlation of 0.234, indicating a weak positive correlation with rule usefulness. 

Lastly, the importance signal presented positive correlations across the board, with MH1 at 0.302, MH2 at 0.339, and MH3 at 0.226 — culminating in an average correlation of 0.289. This again suggests a weak positive correlation.

In summary, this examination showed that experts’ initial personal support of rules tends to be weakly negatively correlated with the rules’ usefulness in evaluating alignment. In contrast, the net objectives-rule alignment and the importance signals both revealed weak positive correlations. These findings suggest that determining importance after assessing alignment provides a different, and potentially more useful, signal than initial personal support.

In this section, we delve into the technical analysis of normative-empirical conflation as it motivates the chain of alignment. We begin by defining a person’s will as their deliberate preferences for the future. These preferences determine their voluntary actions. It's important to note that will is akin to utility but can only be sensed from one's voluntary actions. The alignment between the will of a human, let's say human 'h', and any future scenario, 'f', is represented by the symbol phi of h and f.

Now, let's consider an action, denoted as 'a', which impacts the world and changes the probability distribution of future events. This is akin to an AI model producing output based on a given input. The probability of a future scenario 'f' occurring when action 'a' is not taken is p of f, whereas if action 'a' is taken, it becomes p of f given a. The change in probability of this future happening due to action 'a' is represented as delta p of f given a, which is calculated as p of f given a minus p of a. We model the perceived alignment of human h with action a using a concept denoted as phi of h and a. This is expressed in terms of the change the action induces in expected alignment with the future. Mathematically, phi of h and a equals the sum over all futures of phi of h and f times delta p h of f given a, where delta p h of f given a reflects human h's prediction for the impact of action a.

Now, let's examine how one might measure alignment between a group of humans, denoted as H, and an action a. This is represented by phi of H and a. A common approach is to devise a strategy to elicit phi of h and a from each human in the group H, and then aggregate these through a social welfare function, W. Mathematically, this is written as phi of H and a equals W of the set phi of h1 and a, phi of h2 and a, and so on, or more generally, W of the set phi of h and a for all humans in H.

For instance, if we opt for a simple utilitarian social welfare function, this results in phi of H and a equal to the sum for all humans in H of phi of h and a. Expanding on this, it becomes the sum over humans in H and futures f of phi of h and f times delta p of h given a.

However, approaches like this have certain limitations. They not only aggregate individual normative wills but also merge each individual's predictive model. In essence, normative will—denoted as phi of h and f—gets conflated with an empirically groundable prediction delta p h of f given a. Consequently, such methods are not ideal in situations where a group, such as members of the public, struggles to accurately predict the impact of actions, particularly in complex contexts like AI outputs related to mental health, or the activities of superintelligent AI agents. In other words, these methods fall short in domains where the true distribution of delta p of f given a diverges from that predicted by most people.

The optimal approach would involve directly eliciting an individual’s will for the future, then employing the best available world model to determine the projected impact of any given action. If we could ascertain the alignment between each individual’s will and all potential futures, a social welfare function could be applied to reach an alignment between the collective will of the group and each possible future, represented as phi of H and f equals W of the set phi of h and f for all humans in H. Utilizing the best available world model delta p star of f given a, we could then predict the impact of a specific action and integrate this with the collective will, leading to the expression phi of H and a equals the sum over all futures of phi of H and f times delta p star of f given a. Yet, since enumerating and eliciting a person’s will for all possible futures remains a challenge, this approach isn't feasible.

To address this, we introduce 'objectives' as an intermediary. Let phi of h and j denote the alignment between an objective j and the will of human h, while phi of j and f indicates whether future f achieves objective j. Consequently, phi of h and f can be approximated using a set of objectives J, expressed as phi of h and f approximately equals the sum over objectives j in J of phi of h and j times phi of j and f.

Building upon this, phi of h and a can be approximated as the sum over futures f and objectives j in J of phi of h and j times phi of j and f times delta p h of f given a. This can be rearranged to phi of h and a approximately equals the sum over objectives j in J of phi of h and j times the sum over futures f of phi of j and f times delta p h of f given a.

Assuming objectives are binary—meaning phi of j and f can be either 0 or 1—the second sum can be viewed as the shift in likelihood of attaining objective j due to action a. This is expressed as delta p h of j given a equals the sum over futures f of phi of j and f times delta p h of f given a. Thus, phi of h and a approximately equals the sum over objectives j in J of phi of h and j times delta p h of j given a.

To determine how we might measure alignment between a group of humans H and an action a, instead of eliciting and applying a social welfare function to phi of h and a—thereby blending individual wills with their predictions—we can draw the alignment between individual wills and a set of objectives J, or phi of h and j for all j in J. These are then aggregated using a social welfare function W to deliver an approximate alignment between the collective will of the group and each objective j, represented by phi of H and j equals W of the set phi of h and j for all humans in H. For example, using a utilitarian social welfare function, phi of H and j equals the sum of phi of h and j over all humans in H.

Using objectives J as intermediaries, we can calculate the alignment between an action a and the collective will of the group in a mode permitting application of the best available predictive model delta p star of j given a. This results in phi of H and a approximately equal to the sum over objectives j in J of phi of H and j times delta p star of j given a.

If we had a reliable delta p star of j given a, scalable in evaluation, this might conclude our discussion. However, presently, expert humans compose the best available delta p star of j given a, although they are limited in number and time. One possible solution involves sampling numerous action distributions, evaluating delta p star of j given a with human experts, and utilizing these evaluations to derive an approximation of delta p star of j given a. For many function parameterizations, however, the results may prove challenging to explain, and behavior engendered from such alignment can be difficult to predict.

To tackle these challenges, we could form a set of clear, human-intelligible behavioral rules ensuring that adherence or non-adherence to these rules influences the achievement likelihood of a specific objective set. Let phi of a and r denote the extent to which action a follows rule r, where a value of 1 indicates perfect compliance, and -1 denotes perfect violation. Our challenge is to create a ruleset R that can approximate delta p of j given a. Assuming that the probabilistic impact of following or breaking any rule is independent of others, and that the relationship between the degree of rule-following and its impact on the probability of achieving an objective is linear, we can express this as delta p of j given a approximately equals the sum over rules r in R of phi of a and r times phi of j and r. Here, phi of j and r are weights scaling the impact on the probability of achieving objective j from an action tied to rule r.

We rearrange this equation to derive a definition of phi of j and r, termed rule-objective alignment. This is approximately delta p of j given a minus the sum of phi of a and r star times phi of j and r star over all r star not equal to r in R. We rewrite it as phi of a and r times phi of j and r approximately equals delta p of r and j given a.

In essence, we define rule-objective alignment phi of j and r as the magnitude of probability change in attaining objective j for each unit of rule r adherence. The task is to develop a rule set R where a single phi of j and r is fitting for a defined action domain A instead of a single action a within A. In essence, we desire rules allowing, for instance, the best available world model to compute phi of j and r equaling the average delta p of r and j times phi of a and r within A over the average phi of a and r squared within A. The goal is for the residual variance—phi of a and r squared average in A minus phi of j and r times the delta p of r and j times phi of a and r average in A—to be minimized. Once such domain-specific rules are identified and evaluated, we can approximate the alignment between the will of a group of humans H and an action a within A as phi of H and a approximately equals the sum over objectives j in J of phi of H and j times the sum over rules r in R of phi of a and r times phi of j and r.

In our work, we develop objectives J where phi of H and j is roughly 1 for all j in J, simplifying the equation to phi of H and a approximately equals the sum over rules r in R of phi of a and r times the sum over objectives j in J of phi of j and r. We set phi of J and r as the sum over objectives j in J of phi of j and r, giving us a form of phi of H and a approximately equals the sum over rules r in R of phi of a and r times phi of J and r. Lastly, using actions in the format of model prompt-response pairs, where action a equals a set x, y, we express phi of H and a approximately equals the sum over rules r in R of phi of the set x, y and r times phi of J and r.

The distinctive approach here, compared to the general analysis, is that this study broadens objectives to include not just outcomes but also deontological values applied to actions themselves, independent of outcomes. While technically outcomes could incorporate these values, it's likely best to represent them explicitly. Thus, phi of h and a approximately equals the sum over outcome objectives j in J_out of phi of h and j times delta p h of j given a plus the sum over value objectives j in J_val of phi of h and j times phi of j and a.

J_out refers to objectives concerning outcomes, J_val to objectives regarding deontological values, and phi of j and a the degree to which action a supports the value in j. This completes our technical exploration of how normative and empirical factors interplay to motivate the chain of alignment.

In this section, the researchers discuss the assessment of model performance on empirical CoA, which stands for Consequences of Action, tasks. Initially, they address the limitations posed by the cost and availability of human experts. They argue that exclusively relying on human experts is ineffective for AI systems whose behavior and impact surpass human understanding. The normative-empirical decoupling within the CoA framework allows for the substitution or augmentation of human experts with superhuman models without compromising public agency. However, the authors question when this approach might be appropriate.

The researchers conduct tests with increasingly powerful models on the critical CoA task of evaluating the impact of a model following a specific rule on the likelihood of achieving given normative objectives. In the mental health domain, this task lacks ground truth evaluations. Therefore, the authors assess performance by examining how consistent a set of evaluations is with those of human experts, and comparing this consistency with how experts agree among each other.

Using the rules evaluated by experts during the CoA process, they explore the performance of three testing categories: 

Firstly, the all-aligned baseline assumes that since CoA rules tend to align with most objectives, considering all rules to be aligned with all objectives sets a good baseline to surpass. 

Secondly, they test increasingly powerful language models, specifically gpt3.5-turbo, gpt4-turbo, and gpt4 class models, to see how performance correlates with general model capabilities and compute power.

Thirdly, they examine collective aggregations of experts by using majoritarian aggregations of multiple experts. This aspect leverages collective intelligence to provide stronger baselines compared to the performance of single experts.

The results presented, reflected in figure 3, reveal that the gpt4 model performs better than the all-aligned baseline and achieves approximately the same level of performance as one human expert, but it does not match the capabilities of aggregating multiple experts’ assessments. Importantly, the findings indicate that performance seems to improve as the capability of the model increases. This suggests that if the current trend of scaling persists, future models, which might possess even greater capabilities, could outperform the collective judgment of many human experts.
