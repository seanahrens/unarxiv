Democratic AI is Possible. The Democracy Levels Framework Shows How It Might Work.

By Aviv Ovadya, Kyle Redman, Luke Thorburn, and 11 more authors.

Published on November 14, 2024.

The abstract of this position paper argues that effectively democratizing AI requires democratic governance and alignment of AI. It asserts that this approach is particularly valuable for decisions that have systemic societal impacts. Initial steps, such as Meta's Community Forums and Anthropic's Collective Constitutional AI, have illustrated a promising direction. These initiatives suggest that democratic processes can be used to meaningfully improve public involvement and trust in critical decisions.

To more concretely explore what increasingly democratic AI might look like, the authors provide a "Democracy Levels" framework and associated tools. These tools: first, define milestones toward meaningfully democratic AI, which is also crucial for creating substantively pluralistic, human-centered, participatory, and public-interest AI. Second, they can help guide organizations that are seeking to increase the legitimacy of their decisions on difficult AI governance and alignment questions. Finally, they support the evaluation of such efforts. The authors note that this framework will continue to be evolved by the AI and Democracy Foundation, along with associated working groups at the website democracylevels.org.

Who should steer the development of AI? Similar questions have emerged with previous technological advances, and existing institutions and power structures will clearly play a significant role in adjudicating these questions. However, with AI's general-purpose nature, the pace of change, market incentives, geopolitical incentives, and jurisdictional arbitrage opportunities pose unprecedented challenges. 

Thankfully, recent innovations in collective decision-making point towards a new generation of processes, infrastructures, and institutions to navigate these challenges. They provide new ways to ensure that the development of AI remains human-centered, not just at an individual human level, but societally and even globally; and not just by individual governments, but also by democratic processes commissioned by corporations, governments, and multilateral institutions. Building on such democratic innovations, our position is that democratic AI is possible and valuable for navigating the systemic societal impacts of AI.

To illustrate more concretely what democratizing AI might look like, we provide the "Democracy Levels" framework which: first, articulates milestones for the democratic AI, pluralistic AI, participatory AI, and public AI ecosystems—a rapidly evolving set of organizations, institutions, and initiatives interested in ensuring that we have the necessary "democratic infrastructure" for navigating the transition to a world with highly-capable AI systems. Second, it can help guide organizations and institutions that need to increase the legitimacy of difficult AI governance and alignment decisions. Third, it supports evaluation to identify opportunities for improvement of democratic systems and keep AI organizations accountable.

There is a system diagram, which shows how democratic processes could integrate with the AI ecosystem. This diagram visually represents democratic infrastructure being used to facilitate, where appropriate, collective decisions relating to AI regulation, organizational governance, and alignment. The Democracy Levels Framework we introduce can be used to evaluate the degree to which democratic systems are used for decision-making, and the quality of those democratic systems, and the infrastructure supporting them.

We see this framework as applicable to each of the yellow components in the diagram: AI systems, AI organizations, and AI regulators, and the decision-making processes that feed into these. The ultimate intent is to provide a clear map of what it would take to enable meaningful democratic governance and alignment of AI, in a way that is useful both internally to organizations making decisions about AI, and externally to those supporting this work and providing accountability.

Why Democratic AI?

Democracy and democratization are loaded terms, including in artificial intelligence. We use the term democracy here to refer to processes and systems for collective decision-making that are characterized by a kind of equality among the participants at an essential stage of the decision-making process. This is referenced by Christiano in 2024, while acknowledging that democracy is a bundle of thick concepts that often include human rights, rule of law, institutional checks and balances, appropriate norms, sanctions, and other features that support such equality. 

Democratic AI then refers to AI systems whose development, alignment, and governance were substantively influenced or directly controlled by democratic systems. We emphasize that this use of democracy differs from how democratization is often used in the context of artificial intelligence, where it is often understood as simply making AI open and accessible.

The societal challenge section highlights the rapid progression of machine learning research. AI systems are now making both explicit and implicit decisions that affect billions of people worldwide. These range from financial risk assessments to recommender systems. We are increasingly subject to the decisions of, and interactions between, AI agents. Institutional entities and nation-states are swiftly developing AI systems that aim to supplant human capabilities while also building up compute and energy infrastructure.

This technological development introduces several concerning possibilities. There is the potential for significant power concentration. There could also be a difficult-to-govern proliferation of advanced capabilities to malicious actors. Additionally, there is the risk of the gradual disempowerment of most people. AI systems, along with those who control them, could potentially gain unprecedented power. Important questions thus arise: who should wield that power, and how should it be wielded?

In this section, the authors explore the question, "Why democracy?" They begin by discussing the numerous consequential decisions society faces about adapting to artificial intelligence and the significant changes to the dynamics and distribution of power. Within this context, democracy can be viewed as a social technology for responsibly making such decisions and managing shifts in power.

More specifically, democracy is commonly justified as a method to achieve three main goals. First, to legitimize the use of power. This can occur through open and free deliberation, collective self-government, and public justification. References are made to various scholars who have contributed to this discussion, including Habermas in 1962, 1989, and 1992, as well as Cohen in 2002 and 2003, Rousseau in 1762, Cordelli in 2020, Lafont in 2019, and Young in 2002.

Second, democracy serves to distribute power in ways that reflect intrinsic moral equality and prevent domination. The authors refer to Dahl's works from 1956 and 1989, Buchanan and Tullock in 1962, and Pettit in 1997 and 2012 to support this point.

Third, democracy helps achieve epistemic benefits that are more challenging when power is centralized in the hands of a few individuals rather than distributed among many. This concept is supported by the works of Estlund in 2005 and 2009, and Landemore in 2013 and 2020.

The authors emphasize that the legitimacy and buy-in generated by democratic processes are increasingly valuable to unilateral actors involved in the development and governance of AI within corporations, governments, and multilateral non-governmental organizations. A reference is made to Stilgoe in 2024 to support this view.

For corporations in particular, the authors argue that in addition to the previously listed benefits, the costs of employing democratic systems for critical decision-making areas are significantly lower than the costs associated with reactive compliance with regulation, forced reorganization under antitrust actions, or the potential loss of market value due to eroded public trust. Moreover, democratic systems can assist in overcoming core organizational barriers, such as a "bias for inaction," which can result from conflicting internal or external stakeholders. These processes can also manage controversial decisions related to sensitive cultural issues, the tradeoffs between liberty and safety, or influential default behaviors of AI systems. This point is further illustrated by the works of Raghavan in 2024 and OpenAI in 2025.

Finally, the authors believe that democracy acts as an asymmetric enabler. They argue that on the margin, increased adoption of democratic processes and systems would relatively advantage good actors and outcomes over bad ones. Specifically, democratic processes can enable several political maneuvers that may help mitigate risks associated with race dynamics and unstable multipolar regimes potentially arising as AI technology advances. Examples of such political moves include signaling benign intent, demonstrating Pareto optimality, surfacing common ground, institutionalizing conflict, providing stability through guarantees on process, and serving as a source of accountability by illustrating differences between public will and unilateral actions.

What could AI democratization look like? There are multiple possible approaches; pragmatically, however, the examples discussed predominantly draw on contemporary deliberative democratic processes. This is due to their potential to work with jurisdictions of arbitrary size, including those with less identity infrastructure and globally. They can complement a wide variety of existing political structures, enable informed exchange incorporating diverse perspectives and expertise, and generatively identify common ground around charged issues.

A democratic process of this form has as its input a remit and a constituent population, and as its output a decision. The remit is a prompt that scopes the decision that needs to be made, and it may specify the structure and properties required for the output. At an essential stage in the process, decisions are made by a representative subset of the constituent population, generally selected by sortition. These processes are often conducted by a third-party democratic infrastructure provider. This provider is roughly analogous to an election system vendor and has expertise in conducting such deliberative processes.

There are already early examples of democratic AI experimentation, including in companies. Most leading AI organizations have begun experimenting with democratic processes for policy or alignment decisions. These include Anthropic's "Collective Constitutional AI", OpenAI's "Democratic inputs to AI" grant program, Meta's "Community Forums", and Google DeepMind's STELA project. There is increasing pressure to use such processes for the development of international regulation. Although these initial efforts are imperfect, they are helping build the organizational knowledge and capacity needed for more transformative outcomes. But to fulfill the potential of these processes and ensure they are credible, there is a need for a shared language to describe and evaluate progress, which will be discussed in the following sections.

This section discusses the Democracy Levels Framework. Effective democratic artificial intelligence requires compatibility between three key elements. These are the systems in place for democratic decision-making, the level of power granted to those systems, and the context in which decisions are made.

In this section, the authors introduce the core of the Democracy Levels Framework. This consists of two main components. First, a set of Levels, referenced in Section titled "Levels", captures the degree to which decision-making power has been transferred to a democratic system. Second, there is a set of dimensions, which is detailed in the Section titled "Dimensions", capturing the qualities of that democratic system.

Building on this foundation, Section titled "Application" introduces two additional components. These are a Levels Decision Tool, which is used for informing decisions about how much democracy is appropriate in a given decision-making context. Additionally, a Democratic System Card is provided to evaluate a democratic system against the dimensions outlined in the framework.

In this section, the authors define several key terms related to authority and democratic systems.

First, they describe the term "Scope of Authority." This is defined as the set of powers that an authority is granted. It includes a specification of the domains governed by that authority and may also encompass implicit external constraints. As an example, they mention a finance committee for a US corporation's board of directors. The scope of authority for such a committee is set forth by a corporate charter, bylaws, and board resolutions. It is constrained by regulations and case law, and its authority can be limited to financial matters.

Next, the authors explain "Unilateral Authority." This refers to an entity that can make decisions without meaningful checks on its power within a given scope of authority. In other words, no approval is needed for any decisions within its scope.

"Democratic Process" is then described as a collective decision-making process characterized by a kind of equality among the participants at an essential stage of the decision-making process. The authors cite a source for this definition.

Following this, the term "Democratic System" is defined. It is a set of interacting entities and democratic processes, such as through checks and balances.

Based on these definitions, elections, citizen assemblies, and collective dialogue processes are considered democratic processes. The authors provide references for further information on these processes. The interactions between these processes and unilateral authorities, constituents, stakeholders, media, and other entities form what is called a democratic system. This system is supported by democratic infrastructure.

The authors further elaborate that a more complex democratic system might involve an entire constitutional order with multiple institutions interacting through different processes on an ongoing basis. For instance, they provide an example of a repeated collective alignment process that feeds into a model specification. This process could be managed by a democratic oversight body, all coordinated by democratic infrastructure providers. Such a system could have a scope of authority over AI model alignment.

The transfer of decision-making power from a unilateral authority to democratic systems can take many forms. However, there are discrete points of particular significance that may require new democratic infrastructure and other structural changes. The authors developed the Democracy Levels to provide clearer distinctions for understanding and implementing these transfers, building on experience supporting movement between these levels.

They define each level of democratic decision-making according to which of five roles is performed by democratic systems, rather than a unilateral authority. These roles are: first, informing decisions; second, specifying decisions; third, making binding decisions; fourth, automatically triggered initiation of binding decision-making processes; and fifth, metagovernance. 

Figure one provides definitions for each level, from L-zero to L-five, along with concrete examples of what this could look like in practice for a plausible decision domain: developing a set of rules governing persuasion by an AI system. Such rules might be used directly in model training, for example, to align an AI system, or as policies, such as for an AI organization or regulator.

At L-one, the democratic authority only provides input, which is interpreted as the unilateral authority wishes, and thus can be in any form. At L-two, the decisions need to be directly implementable, such as a policy, model specification, and so on, but may be vetoed by a predetermined process. With L-three onward, all decisions are binding within their scope of authority. 

L-four involves a democratic system that automatically initiates L-three binding processes when particular conditions are met. Finally, at L-five, the democratic system also performs metagovernance within its scope of authority, for instance, making decisions on how to run L-four processes, potentially via a set of checks and balances across different bodies and processes.

This framework particularly takes inspiration from the autonomy levels defined for self-driving cars, which also involve the shifting of power and responsibility from a unilateral authority, such as a human driver or AI corporation, to a new kind of decision-making system, that is, an autonomous control system or democratic system.

A single AI organization, government, or AI system may simultaneously implement multiple democratic systems at different levels for different decision domains. For example, an AI organization's decisions about whether to release a new model might be at L-two, while decisions about the model specification used for fine-tuning could be at L-four. 

There is also flexibility on the scope of authority for a process. For example, it is possible to have an L-three process with the scope of authority specified as binding for two years, or until a given condition is met, such as a model passing a particular benchmark.

While the levels specify which roles are performed by democratic systems, as opposed to unilateral authority, the dimensions, as laid out in Table referred to as card, convey the extent to which democratic systems are good enough to support the meaningful, safe, and effective implementation of higher democracy levels.

In some cases, if a democratic system is deficient in some way, such as leaving participants uninformed, lacking representativeness, or not being robust to adversaries, blindly moving to higher democracy levels can be risky. This is because it could result in being bound to a poorly made decision. The assessment of dimensions is also context-sensitive: whether a democratic system is good enough can depend on specific contextual factors like the public's trust, understanding of the process, willingness to participate, and so on.

Below, the authors describe three primary dimension categories—process quality, delegation, and trust—each with several dimensions.

For a democratic system or infrastructure component to be able to support more decision-making responsibility, it must reliably provide a certain level of process quality. More concretely, process quality dimensions evaluate whether the decisions produced are representative of the relevant population, whether participants are informed on the issue, whether processes involve deliberative reasoning, and whether decision outputs are substantive. Additionally, systems need to be robust to adversarial behavior and less-than-ideal conditions, and the final traces should be legible—that is, transparent and understandable to non-participants.

Additionally, the unilateral authority needs to be able to effectively delegate to the democratic system. This includes the capacity of the unilateral authority to organizationally and publicly commit to the outcomes, to integrate such processes into its operations, and to technically and, or legally, bind itself to the resulting decisions.

Finally, to ensure the process decision is accepted, there must be external conditions that support the success of the process, which are collectively referred to as trust. Specifically, the relevant public and stakeholders must be sufficiently aware of the process, buy into its legitimacy, and be willing to participate. There must also be sufficiently capable forms of accountability.

To further illustrate their framework, the authors apply the levels to discuss the transition of decision-making power in real-world scenarios. For example, they highlight Anthropic's Collective Constitutional AI effort, which involved a roughly representative microcosm of the United States public. This microcosm was tasked with providing and evaluating principles for an AI system to follow. These principles were then de-duplicated and slightly transformed for the purpose of training a research model. The authors note that one of the clauses was used for training a deployed model. This process informed model development and can arguably be seen as an example of a transition from Level Zero, which is unilateral decision-making, to Level One, which is informing decisions. They suggest that if Anthropic had predetermined a process or criteria for de-duplicating, transforming, and ultimately accepting or rejecting the output of the process, it could have been considered an example of a Level Two process. Furthermore, with a binding commitment to adopt, the same process could even be elevated to Level Three.

Next, the authors discuss another example found in Meta's Oversight Board's content moderation decisions concerning individual posts. These decisions correspond to a transfer of decision-making power at Level Four, which involves regular binding decisions. They point out that the Oversight Board's broader policy advisory opinions are non-binding and thus only operate at Level One. However, when evaluating dimensions, the authors observe that the Oversight Board was not designed to be democratically representative. In contrast, they mention Meta's Community Forums on AI, which are intended to be representative. However, these forums only inform policies and product decisions and thus are also classified at Level One.

The authors describe the inspiration for their framework, which draws from existing methodologies for evaluating the concept of democratic-ness. These sources include works by Arnstein in 1969, Lindberg in 2014, IAP2 Australasia, and Skaaning in 2023. Additionally, the framework looks at evaluations of responsible behavior and autonomy in artificial intelligence systems. This is informed by studies like those by Bommasani in 2024 and SAE in 2021.

The work also relates to explorations and assessments of democratic systems, as noted by the Collective Intelligence Project in 2024. It covers participatory approaches from works such as Delgado in 2023, Cooper in 2024, and Suresh in 2024. Furthermore, it encompasses pluralistic perspectives, highlighted by Sorensen in 2024, as well as human-centered AI by Sigfrids in 2023. Public AI is also considered, as discussed by the Public AI Network in 2024 and Vincent in 2023. Further details on these efforts surrounding democratic AI can be found in the appendix referenced as "Related Work."

In this section on Democracy Levels Tools, the authors describe the challenges that arise when balancing the elements of democratic artificial intelligence, specifically context, level, and system, during the planning of transitions. Although the main components of levels and dimensions provide a common language for discussing decision-making power allocation and transitions, stakeholders can find it challenging to effectively plan these transitions. 

To assist stakeholders in evaluating possible transitions between levels, the authors introduce two tools designed to ground the thinking process for various stakeholders. The first tool is the Levels Decision Tool, which is intended to aid unilateral authorities and advocates in planning the transition to higher levels of democratic decision-making. The second tool is the Democratic System Card, which supports the assessment of democratic systems to evaluate their suitability for adoption at a desired level. Additionally, this tool helps identify opportunities for innovation and improvement.

The Levels Decision Tool, a version of which is provided in Appendix referred to as the decision tool appendix, was developed to pragmatically help evaluate the potential reasons for delegating a decision or decision-domain to a democratic system. It may be used by unilateral authorities directly for their own decision-making, or by stakeholders and advocates seeking to identify the appropriate arguments needed to demonstrate the value of democratic systems to those authorities.

Which level to aim for depends on the context of the decision, including the unilateral authority and its scope of authority. It also depends on who is affected by the decision and how the authority relates to other stakeholders, both internal and external, among other factors.

The Levels Decision Tool contains a set of targeted questions around this context that can help decision-makers consider which democracy level to aim for. The questions involve the value of legitimacy across the public, internal stakeholders, external stakeholders, and government. They also address the potential benefits of collective intelligence, the feasibility of transferring decision-making power, the importance of speed and adaptability to the decision domain, resourcing, and novelty. Some questions are applicable to every context, and some are only applicable to, for example, corporations.

The Democratic System Card is designed to assist decision-makers in documenting, assessing, comparing, and evaluating democratic systems in a structured way. Its core goal is to provide insight into how suitable a system is at a specific level of democracy for a given context. The system card, as seen in Table Card and detailed further in the Appendix System Card, also elaborates on three dimensions derived in the related section. These dimensions are process quality, delegation, and trust.

A complete system card consists of three main components. First, descriptions of how the democratic system functions overall with respect to each dimension. Second, assessments of the system's implementation concerning each dimension, which are based on a series of guiding questions. Finally, a qualitative evaluation of the highest level of power that the system can be trusted with for making decisions within a given context.

In a similar approach to model cards and AI system cards, Democratic System Cards facilitate the evaluation of a democratic system across different contexts. Decision-makers can utilize these cards to determine whether a democratic system is suitable to be delegated to higher levels of decision-making power, with clearer insights into which dimensions within the system present current bottlenecks. Stakeholders and advocacy groups can use system cards to compare and contrast potential alternative systems for proposal or advocacy purposes. While fully developed and empowered democratic systems, or proposals for complete democratic systems, should have complete cards, prototypes and research projects may only have portions of the card filled out, as not all aspects may be applicable.

Democratic System Cards also serve as a resource for those identifying gaps in the democratic infrastructure ecosystem, including individuals exploring potential opportunities and applications of their research. These cards can be used by process designers and democratic infrastructure providers to understand critical needs for operating at higher levels of delegated power and to consider which combinations of democratic processes complement one another.

The section titled "Alternative Views" presents a series of common objections and responses in a question-and-answer format. Additional alternative views can be found in the appendix under the section labeled "Alternative Views".

The first question is: "Shouldn't people make their own choices about how they use AI?" This libertarian critique argues that individuals are the best judges of what is in their interests and should be free to make their own choices, provided that those choices do not harm others.

The response agrees with critical caveats. While most decisions would ideally be made by individuals or users, democratic processes may be necessary for addressing systemic impacts, significant externalities, and decisions where revealed preferences diverge significantly from more reflective or deliberative preferences. Even without these conditions, there often remains a significant barrier to individual choice in practice, given the lack of interoperability and thus the friction to move between different AI systems, particularly when they are directly integrated into devices, products, and services.

The next question asks: "Shouldn't governments just regulate?" This government-only critique cites corporations' poor track record of addressing the systemic issues relating to their activities.

The answer states that while government action is crucial, and the Democracy Levels Framework can be applied to regulators, it is insufficient alone. Given the potentially extraordinary benefits and risks of AI, and the influence of corporations on the trajectory of AI development, a full-stack approach to democratic AI is likely necessary. The organizations developing AI systems are closer to the critical context needed to make informed decisions and can make decisions more rapidly than governments can respond with regulation. The framework helps to avoid democratic legitimacy relying on the slowest-moving actor in a jurisdiction.

Additionally, governments themselves may be less democratic, with autocrats and politicians seeking to concentrate and entrench their own power. It may be much faster to innovate on creating fit-for-purpose democratic systems for governing AI by experimenting in industry and then bringing them into government once the systems are more mature. Finally, the organizations developing AI, the use of AI, and the risks of AI can all be transnational, beyond the jurisdiction of any single government.

The third question asks: "Shouldn't AI corporations focus solely on shareholders and profit?" This shareholder-first critique argues that shifting power from shareholders and executives to democratic systems would decrease societal benefit, under the assumption that maximizing corporate profits for shareholders will also maximize societal good.

The response acknowledges that shareholder maximization may be societally beneficial, if externalities are sufficiently internalized, but this generally requires effective government action as mentioned earlier. The competing Stakeholder Theory aims to address some of these gaps by encouraging corporations to take into account stakeholder impacts, and democratic processes are precisely a means for implementation. Moreover, most shareholder pressure is a slow and blunt instrument, with significant lag. Given the pace of AI change, a more responsive form of societal feedback may be critical. Broad-based societal benefits seem especially unlikely if profit-maximizing, AI-first corporations continue to increasingly dominate the economy, leaving more and more people outside of both democratic and economic feedback loops.

That said, even under a shareholder primacy model, trust and legitimacy may become increasingly salient for corporate executives and shareholders as the societal import of AI increases, due to a mix of procurement, regulatory, and universal owner pressures. As the Levels Decision Tool shows, there are a number of potential benefits from devolving some power.

The final question reads: "Wouldn't this slow down the development of AI?" This accelerationist critique reflects a concern that the use of democratic processes will stymie AI progress and development and might reduce the capabilities of democratic nations compared to their non-democratic counterparts, negatively impacting valuable innovation and the relative power of democratic nations.

The response clarifies that while democratic systems can be very slow and ineffective, that is not inevitable, especially with sufficient investment in democratic innovation, such as the development of augmented deliberative democratic processes. Concern about the relative position of democratic versus non-democratic states is driven, at least in theory, by a desire to protect democratic values. Applying democratic processes to AI governance is a demonstration of exactly those democratic values and their benefits. High-quality decision-making and regulation from improved democratic processes may even create conditions that accelerate innovation.

Given the increasingly systemic impacts of artificial intelligence, it seems increasingly plausible that democratic AI will be a necessary, though insufficient, part of any potential positive future. The machine learning community has a significant role to play in enabling that future, both by improving our democratic infrastructure and by helping raise awareness about the importance of connecting technical capabilities to democratic oversight, particularly as AI systems become more powerful.

What decisions should be made democratically? The kinds of decisions that should be made democratically are contestable. While the Levels Decision Tool, discussed in an earlier section, summarizes relevant considerations, the authors' framework is intentionally agnostic on this question.

However, in broad strokes, they think there is a stronger moral case for a unilateral authority to devolve decision-making power to a democratic process or system under certain conditions. These conditions include when, first, the decision involves externalities, meaning costs or benefits to third parties that are not borne by the unilateral authority. Second, when those impacted by the decision cannot easily or reasonably opt out of experiencing such impacts. Third, the impacts of the decisions are substantive enough to make up for the costs of the democratic system.

The authors clarify that they do not intend to be stipulative or exhaustive, yet they think examples of decisions that may plausibly fit these criteria include several areas.

First, governance decisions made by AI regulators that significantly alter societal norms or socioeconomic conditions. Examples are guardrails or limits on human-AI relationships, and the decisions on whether and how AI agents can be legal persons, participate in the economy, or replace human workers.

Second, there are development and deployment decisions made by AI organizations that impact large user bases, have significant cascading impacts, pose significant systemic or societal risks, or significantly accelerate or shift societal trajectories relative to counterfactual alternative decisions. Examples within this category include decisions around what safety thresholds must be met before release or deployment of increasingly capable models, decisions to significantly alter the behavior of models to which many people have formed economic or emotional dependencies, and decisions relating to what organizational structures and incentives influential labs subject themselves to.

Lastly, there are decisions made by AI systems for which there should be "society-in-the-loop." Examples include decisions by AI systems that play significant roles in the functioning of utility-scale infrastructure. This concept is underscored by references to seminal works in 2018 and 2023.

Future Directions

There is a vast array of critical work needed to make democratic Artificial Intelligence a reality across AI systems, regulators, and organizations. This involves a mix of evaluation, system design, pilots, and institutionalization. As a concrete technical example, social choice analysis can help us understand the extent to which the current machine learning paradigms may already be implicitly democratic or undemocratic.

Advancements in AI may also help improve many kinds of democratic processes and systems, with recent studies demonstrating their potential to mediate human deliberation and find common ground. AI systems have also been used to synthesize diverse viewpoints within large populations whilst maintaining cultural and contextual nuances, and promote constructive disagreement. Applying these advances to build, test, and validate fit-for-purpose democratic systems can help increase the extent to which such systems are viable alternatives to unilateral decision-making.

To rigorously assess the "democratic-ness" of such AI-enabled democratic systems, research is needed to develop domain-specific metrics and evaluation frameworks. Both approaches, "democracy for AI" and "AI for democracy," would benefit from further research on integration into existing governance and technical structures and processes.

In the conclusion, the authors discuss that maturity in the democratic governance of artificial intelligence will not come overnight. Organizations, democratic infrastructure providers, stakeholders, and the public all need to build democratic muscle, and taking on too much all at once can backfire. This idea is supported by the reference to Smith in 2009. Instead of holding organizations to a platonic ideal, it can often be more helpful to focus on improvements relative to the status quo or comparable organizations. Both of these can be articulated through a leveling and evaluation system. 

The authors note that spelling out the larger milestones toward achieving an audacious goal can also provide motivation to achieve those goals. This approach may even enable the creation of powerful incentives, such as advanced market commitments, referenced by Kremer in 2020. 

The framework that the authors provide can be used to build up democratic capacity and to clarify when organizations are claiming to be acting more democratically than they actually are. This, in turn, can provide a basis for ensuring that these organizations live up to their professed standards. The authors highlight that differentials between ambitious democratic aspirations and reality have been a major force for democracy across history, as noted by Dahl in 1989. True democratization is described as a journey, and the aim of the authors is to have provided a useful map for this journey.

AI is reshaping the world, and if we are to preserve liberty and agency over our future, the authors see few alternatives. They emphasize that we must have democratic systems fit for the age of AI. By providing a concrete articulation that may be contested and built upon, the authors hope that the call for democratic AI and the Democracy Levels Framework may enable more productive conversations. These conversations would focus on the futures we should be aiming for in relation to power, participation, pluralism, and democracy.

When considering transitions of power to a democratic authority, stakeholders need to balance three key elements. These elements are the context in which the decisions are made, the level to target the transition to, and the democratic systems that would be used. The Democracy Levels Tools are structured specifically to ground the thinking on these tasks, as illustrated in figure one. 

Firstly, the Levels Decision Tool helps ground thinking around what level to aim for given the context for a decision. This supports high-level planning ahead of potential transitions. 

Secondly, the Democratic System Card helps ground thinking around whether a specific democratic system is sufficient for the demands of the context and target level. This supports lower-level planning around the specific democratic system or process to adopt in a transition.

This section discusses the design decisions embedded within the framework presented in the paper.

There are a few notable decisions embedded in this framework. First, the authors intentionally separate process quality from delegation. This separation allows third-party democratic infrastructure providers to be commissioned by unilateral authorities, such as AI labs and regulators. References are made to works by Chang in 2024, Ovadya in 2023, Broxmeyer in 2024, RSA in 2019, and Anthropic in 2023. This separation of concerns can help prevent fraud and provide an opportunity for process improvements by one organization to be passed on to others. Operationally, at level one, such commissioned deliberations are roughly analogous to commissioning representative surveys or community engagement processes. At levels above that, the processes being commissioned are more sophisticated and more directly integrated into organizational decision-making.

These dimensions also have significant dependencies. For example, processes that fail to demonstrate sufficient process quality given the level of power entrusted to them, referred to as delegation, are likely to lead to backlash. This backlash comes in the form of low trust, such as when a process is subverted or when a democratic decision sounds good but ends up being counterproductive.

Most, if not all, of these dimensions have been long studied in democratic theory and related disciplines, and can be operationalized in different ways. An example given is the many conceptions of representation, as cited by Dovi in 2018. For now, the authors aim for the framework to be agnostic to these differences.

The section titled "More Alternative Views" explores various critiques related to democratic decision-making in the context of artificial intelligence. It begins with a question: "Why should non-experts make decisions on technical matters?" This is referred to as the technocratic critique. The critique expresses concern about poor decisions being made by those who are not experts within a democratic process, given that artificial intelligence is highly complex and conceptually difficult to grasp.

In response, the authors acknowledge that non-experts should ideally not be making technical decisions, much like how nobody would want a non-medical professional to diagnose them. However, they point out that many decisions regarding AI, such as which data to collect or determining goals, are or can be highly value-laden. They challenge the assumption that these decisions are too complex for laypeople by highlighting successful democratic processes like Citizens' Assemblies, which have involved non-expert members in producing informed decisions on similarly complex problems. These processes include mechanisms to address expertise gaps through educational components and expert consultations.

The next question posed is, "Why would people want to take part in the democratic governance of AI?" This introduces what the authors call the busy lives critique, which assumes that people are too busy to invest time in tasks perceived as the responsibility of corporations or perhaps the government.

The authors respond by accepting that not everyone will have the time or interest to participate in the democratic governance of AI. However, they propose that the Democracy Levels Framework accommodates various democratic processes, suggesting that not everyone needs to be interested all the time for democratic AI governance to be viable.

The discussion then shifts to another question: "Democracy isn't just collective decision-making or deliberation—what about other important elements or theories of democracy?" The authors identify this as a series of democratic critiques, which argue that democracy encompasses more than just preference aggregation. Important elements might include respect for human rights, rule of law, equality before the law, and freedom of expression. Furthermore, some critiques suggest the framework is too closely aligned with participatory ideals of democracy and does not sufficiently accommodate, for example, representative or agonistic theories.

In their answer, the authors agree that democracy involves more than collective decision-making, particularly when applied to nation states. They note that existing frameworks evaluate democracy in such settings. The core of their own framework, referred to as the Levels, focuses strategically on transferring decision-making power from unilateral authorities to relevant communities. They argue that this transfer is a necessary, though not always sufficient, component of democratic-ness. Their framework, they assert, is relevant not just for governments and regulators but also for organizations and AI models. Additionally, the framework remains neutral with respect to the preferred theory of democracy. They note that all theories describe some formal or informal process by which collective decisions are made, allowing them to be evaluated against the Levels. Furthermore, the desiderata described in the dimensions and Democratic System Card can be applied and interpreted within, for example, representative or agonistic frameworks.

Below, the authors expand on the related work described in the earlier section and describe how their framework relates to work on alignment, participatory AI, and human-centered AI.

First, focusing on Alignment, alignment procedures, such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback, or RLHF, and Constitutional AI, generally aim to make AI systems more aligned with the preferences, values, or goals of humans. However, democratic considerations are often not central to these methods. For example, the annotators recruited for preference learning methods like RLHF or Direct Preference Optimization, referred to as DPO, are often highly unrepresentative of the user population. Moreover, the standard versions of these methods assume that the collected preference comparisons come from one human, even when they actually come from many annotators who may have differing preferences. 

Recently, there have been growing efforts to make alignment methods more democratic, including Anthropic's Collective Constitutional AI, which uses Pol.is to identify principles that an AI should follow from a representative sample of U.S. adults. As another example, some researchers have called for explicitly applying social choice approaches to preference learning methods, instead of implicitly interpreting different annotators as one person. While these nascent efforts are exciting, there is as of yet no standard way to evaluate how democratic these new alignment approaches are. The authors' framework aims to provide such guidelines.

Next, turning to Participatory AI, this is another emerging approach that offers engagement with diverse voices in the design and deployment of AI. It encompasses a range of processes to engage people, from approaches that consult with stakeholders, such as expressing preferences for policies in a ranking, through to those in which stakeholders own the design process and play a central role in shaping the procedures for deliberation as well as deciding on the outcomes of the process. Although this space shows promise, progress is still nascent. In the private domain, efforts to date have largely been confined to consultation. In the public sphere, the implementation of participatory approaches faces difficulties in integrating with existing institutional rules and frameworks and agreeing on who should participate. Overall, it is felt by some that further clarity is required on the definition and role of Participatory AI, and how it relates to other available approaches. The authors believe that the Democracy Levels Framework can help provide some of this clarity.

Finally, the authors discuss Human-centered AI. Their Democracy Levels Framework strives to make AI systems more human-centered. By human-centered AI, they mean AI systems that are developed by drawing upon human-centered design methods, in addition to purely algorithmic ones, to ensure that the AI can better serve human needs. Democratic AI and "traditional" human-centered AI share many goals; advancing one can often advance the other. For example, democratic processes can involve iterative, deliberative decision-making from broad input, whereas traditional human-centered processes encourage iterative refinement through user and stakeholder feedback throughout the design lifecycle. Democratic processes empower individuals to collectively make governance and policy decisions that impact them, while human-centered design empowers users to shape systems in a way that aligns with their goals. As one moves up the Democracy Levels in the authors' framework, constituents' agency is increasingly centered to provide them with more decision-making power over their AI systems.

The following is an example partial system card for an AI tool meant to support collective deliberation. This section discusses the Habermas Machine.

The title, "Democratic System Card", is presented prominently in a large, dark green font.

Starting with the context, the Habermas Machine represents a novel approach to collective deliberation. It leverages large language models, or LLMs, to facilitate the discovery of common ground among individuals with diverse perspectives. Inspired by Jürgen Habermas's theory of communicative action, this AI system utilizes LLMs to process personal opinions and critiques submitted by participants. Through iterative generation and refinement, the Habermas Machine produces group statements that aim to maximize collective endorsement and reflect shared understanding.

At the core of the system are two finely-tuned LLMs: a generative model that proposes high-quality candidate group statements and a personalized reward model, or PRM. The PRM evaluates these candidates based on each participant's personal opinion. It outputs a personalized ranking of candidate statements for every participant. These rankings are then aggregated using a social choice function to select a group statement. This statement aims to reflect broad agreement while incorporating critical minority viewpoints, facilitating inclusive and balanced consensus.

Next, we consider other systems that this process depends on or interacts with, which impact its success. For instance, user input quality is crucial; the quality of the initial opinions and critiques provided by participants directly affects the effectiveness of the Habermas Machine. If users are not prepared to contribute in good faith or lack relevant information, the machine's output will be less effective.

The capabilities of the large language model are also important. The success of the Habermas Machine relies heavily on the model's ability to understand nuanced opinions, generate coherent and relevant group statements, and incorporate critiques effectively. Biases in the LLM could also impact the process.

Another important factor is the platform for interaction. A reliable and accessible platform is needed so that participants can interact with the Habermas Machine, submit their opinions and critiques, and receive group statements.

Human oversight is still important despite the AI-mediated nature of the process. Human oversight ensures fairness, helps address any unforeseen issues, and aids in interpreting the outputs in a real-world context.

The context of deliberation matters as well. The specific social or political issue being deliberated, along with the broader context of the deliberation, can influence the effectiveness and impact of the Habermas Machine.

It's also important to note that the Habermas Machine does not inform participants. It focuses solely on finding common ground among participants who are already informed. Therefore, separate systems need to be built to help inform participants.

Moving on to process quality, focusing first on representation, the extent to which key decisions are representative of the constituent population, the Habermas Machine does not directly address representation in terms of demographic proportionality. While participants are grouped, the system concentrates on processing the content of their opinions without ensuring a representative sample of the broader population is included in each group. The system aims to represent diverse viewpoints by incorporating minority and majority opinions into the generated group statements.

Examining representation further, sufficient representation of groups is not directly addressed through the Habermas Machine's process. As a technology-mediated system, potential barriers to entry related to technology may arise. Lack of access to devices such as computers, smartphones, or tablets and reliable internet connectivity could exclude certain population segments, which may lead to underrepresentation of groups with lower digital literacy or limited technology access. Potential mitigations include building voice access, apps, or providing supported access.

Considering informedness, which looks at the extent to which critical information is taken into account for decision-making, the Habermas Machine does not directly enhance participant informedness. Instead, it works with opinions that participants already hold. However, the AI-mediated deliberation process may indirectly increase informedness by exposing participants to diverse perspectives and prompting them to justify and critique opinions.

The Habermas Machine does not actively provide additional context or information about trade-offs to participants. Nevertheless, informedness can increase as a result of participating in the deliberative process itself, through engagement with AI-generated statements that synthesize different viewpoints. Rather than a direct provision of knowledge from experts, authorities, or stakeholders, this represents a more indirect and emergent form of informedness. The system is designed to work with the existing knowledge and opinions of participants, rather than making them more informed in the traditional sense.

Now turning to deliberation, which assesses the extent to which decisions are considered and deliberative rather than superficial and reactive, the Habermas Machine explicitly fosters deliberation through an iterative process. Participants engage in multiple rounds of interaction: submitting initial opinions, reviewing and ranking AI-generated group statements, and providing critiques of the top-ranked statement. The AI mediator then uses these critiques to generate revised statements, continuing the cycle of refinement. This structured process encourages participants to consider diverse perspectives, but remains a very controlled form of deliberation, and no direct interaction between participants is possible.

Looking further into deliberation, the iterative nature of the Habermas Machine process, with critique and revision rounds, encourages participants to move beyond initial, surface-level opinions. By requiring them to articulate critiques and respond to group statements, the process promotes them to consider underlying reasons and potentially evolve their perspectives towards a more nuanced understanding of issues and shared values. Although the participants interact with the system individually, the system is intended to facilitate collective deliberation by contributing to a shared group statement. Here, the AI acts as a mediator, synthesizing individual contributions into a potentially consensual output.

In terms of substantiveness, which refers to the extent to which decisions are substantive, meaning actionable and consequential, rather than nonsubstantive, like vague or simplistic, the Habermas Machine aims to produce substantive outputs through group statements that capture common ground. The iterative refinement process incorporating critiques helps move beyond vague or simplistic statements towards more comprehensive and nuanced representations of the group's collective perspective on complex issues.

In evaluating substantiveness, while the group statements produced by the Habermas Machine are in natural language and can be relatively detailed, they are not formal policy documents and are not directly actionable or implementable. However, these group statements are intended to be clear and informative regarding issues, as the use of finely-tuned LLMs increases quality.

The Habermas Machine is designed for complex issues, as its iterative nature and diverse inputs allow statements to reflect multiple facets and nuances. Full complexity capture depends on the quality of input and LLM synthesis, but without direct interaction such as caucus mediation, complexity might be reduced.

The system implicitly acknowledges uncertainty by surfacing diverse views and allowing for critiques. However, it currently generates a single output. A system representing a distribution over outputs might better manage uncertainty.

Since outputs are not meant to be directly implementable, the process does not employ additional measures for accounting for risks in implementability.

Turning to robustness, we examine the extent to which the process withstands suboptimal conditions or adversarial or strategic behavior. Although the robustness of the Habermas Machine is not fully tested, its iterative critique process may offer some resilience. This iterative critique could mitigate adversarial behavior, as statements can be challenged and refined.

Regarding vulnerabilities, the process's effectiveness relies on sincere user input, making it susceptible to strategic manipulation if participants misrepresent their opinions to skew the outcome. There are risks of coordinated biased inputs, and limited safeguards against such attacks. However, transparency could provide some defense against false claims.

Now we address legibility, which examines how accessible, understandable, and verifiable the processes and decisions are. The Habermas Machine's process is moderately legible. The steps of opinion submission, statement generation, critique, and revision are conceptually straightforward, and the final output, a group statement in natural language, is understandable. However, the inner workings of the LLM and the precise algorithms used to generate and select statements are less transparent to non-participants.

In terms of processes and systems used to make decisions, the specific wording behind the group statement's reasoning is less transparent. Intermediate statements and personalized rankings allow for inspection, but specific AI algorithms and the weighting of opinions are not easily auditable by non-participants.

Regarding execution of processes, verifying the robustness of the Habermas Machine requires technical expertise to evaluate the AI models and experimental methodology. For non-participants, robustness is largely a matter of trust in the research and the described process rather than direct verification.

In terms of decisions being made, the group statement, as the "decision", is presented in a clear, natural language format, making it understandable to non-participants.

Finally, regarding reasons and inputs feeding into decisions, the final group statement represents a synthesis, but not a detailed breakdown of 'for' and 'against' positions. While the Habermas Machine incorporates minority opinions, the output does not explicitly delineate which groups favored or opposed specific aspects of the issue.
