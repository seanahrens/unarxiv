WorldAgents: Can Foundation Image Models be Agents for 3D World Models?.

By Ziya Erkoç, Angela Dai, and Matthias Nießner.

Published on March 20, 2026.

The abstract begins with a question regarding the nature of two-dimensional foundation image models. Specifically, it asks whether these models inherently possess the capabilities to understand and model a three-dimensional world. To explore this, the authors systematically evaluate multiple state-of-the-art image generation models and Vision-Language Models, known as VLMs, on the task of creating three-dimensional worlds.

In order to harness and benchmark the possible implicit 3D capabilities of these models, the authors propose an agentic framing to aid in 3D world generation. Their approach involves a multi-agent architecture. This consists of a VLM-based director, which is responsible for formulating prompts that guide the image synthesis process. Additionally, there is a generator that synthesizes new image views, and a two-step verifier backed by Vision-Language Models. This verifier evaluates and selectively curates the generated frames from both the two-dimensional image and the three-dimensional reconstruction spaces.

Importantly, they demonstrate that this agentic approach provides a coherent and robust 3D reconstruction, resulting in output scenes that can be further explored by rendering new views. Through extensive experiments conducted across various foundation models, the authors show that these two-dimensional models do indeed encapsulate an understanding of three-dimensional worlds. By leveraging this understanding, their method successfully synthesizes expansive, realistic, and three-dimensional-consistent worlds.

Recent rapid advances in two-dimensional foundation models have revolutionized the field of computer vision. Text-to-image diffusion models demonstrate an unprecedented ability to generate high-fidelity, photorealistic images and exhibit deep semantic understanding of visual scenes. Trained on internet-scale datasets, these models encapsulate vast amounts of visual knowledge. 

While two-dimensional generation has reached remarkable heights, the synthesis of immersive, three-dimensional consistent environments, often referred to as three-dimensional world generation, remains a formidable challenge. Existing three-dimensional generation methods are frequently bottlenecked by the scarcity of diverse, high-quality, three-dimensional training data or the computational complexity of maintaining multi-view consistency through Score Distillation Sampling.

Since two-dimensional foundation models are trained on billions of two-dimensional images, each of which represents two-dimensional projections of our three-dimensional spatial world, a compelling hypothesis emerges: these models may have implicitly learned the underlying spatial structures and physical rules of the environments they depict. This leads us to investigate a fundamental question: do two-dimensional foundation image models inherently possess three-dimensional world model capabilities? If these models in fact learn a robust prior of the three-dimensional world, they could theoretically be leveraged to bypass the reliance on explicit three-dimensional datasets, serving as powerful engines for three-dimensional scene synthesis.

To answer this question, we systematically evaluate the implicit three-dimensional spatial understanding of various state-of-the-art image generation models and visual language models, often abbreviated as V L M s. However, high-fidelity three-dimensional reconstruction demands near pixel-perfect cross-view consistency, which single-pass prompting of a two-dimensional model typically fails to guarantee. To harness and benchmark the potential implicit three-dimensional capabilities of such two-dimensional models, we propose a novel agentic method designed to orchestrate two-dimensional foundation models for the task of consistent three-dimensional world generation.

We thus cast three-dimensional scene generation as a multi-agent process, comprising three specialized agents that work together to harness two-dimensional image generation models to reconstruct coherent three-dimensional worlds. The first agent is the V L M Director, which acts as the high-level planner. It dynamically formulates prompts to guide each new image generation and dictates the semantic evolution of the scene. The second agent is the Image Generator, which employs a two-dimensional image generation model that executes spatial navigation by sequentially inpainting to synthesize novel, geometrically aligned views. Since the image models do not have explicit control of the camera positions, we applied an inpainting approach to guide the image model to complete the scene by dictating what to paint. The third agent is the V L M two-stage Verifier, which serves as the critical quality control mechanism. Unlike standard rigid pipelines, this verifier provides fine-grained evaluation to selectively keep or discard generated frames. Crucially, it assesses consistency in two distinct stages: first from the two-dimensional image space only, for semantic and structural coherence, and then from the three-dimensional reconstruction space to guarantee strict geometric alignment.

We found that our agentic approach yields robust three-dimensional reconstructions, allowing us to freely explore various generated environments by rendering arbitrary novel views. Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate that two-dimensional foundation models do, in fact, encapsulate a profound grasp of three-dimensional worlds. By exploiting this latent understanding through our carefully designed multi-agent orchestration, our method overcomes the limitations of independent two-dimensional generation to successfully synthesize expansive, realistic, and strictly three-dimensional-consistent worlds. In summary, our main contributions are as follows: We provide a comprehensive investigation into the implicit three-dimensional world model capabilities of state-of-the-art two-dimensional image generation models guided by V L M s. We introduce a multi-agent architecture comprising a V L M director, a view generator, and a two-step verifier, specifically designed to harness two-dimensional models for consistent three-dimensional synthesis.

Moving on to related works.

The focus on building three-dimensional worlds from text prompts or input views has been gaining traction recently. Various methods have been proposed to leverage the powerful generative capacity of image models or video diffusion models, typically incorporating three-dimensional-based control through camera-controlled conditioning. One such approach is panorama image generation. LayerPano3D employs a layered image generation task by fine-tuning a model called Flux to generate panorama images from text input. Unseen regions of each layer are then inpainted using the same fine-tuned model. Additionally, DreamScene360 incorporates a text-to-image diffusion model to generate a panorama image, while using a self-refinement mechanism that involves a Vision-Language Model, or VLM. 

To make the most of existing image diffusion models, the authors' approach operates entirely without any additional pre-training. They introduce a multi-agent framework that actively guides the entire generation pipeline, rather than just acting as a verifier. Their verification process ensures consistency directly within the three-dimensional reconstruction space, going beyond standard two-dimensional image-domain validation. The generation process employed includes iterative inpainting that does not require generating panorama images.

Another approach to this problem involves both image and depth inpainting. For instance, WonderWorld and Text2Room use hand-crafted prompts to synthesize new regions for creating three-dimensional scenes. In contrast, the authors do not use handcrafted prompts. Instead, they rely on VLM-based agents to orchestrate the scene generation process. This allows them to construct navigable three-dimensional scenes. They also use an iterative, image-based inpainting strategy for scene generation, which shows a high degree of three-dimensional consistency without relying on explicit depth inpainting.

There is also work in scene generation that involves retrieval-based layout-generation methods. These methods depend on three-dimensional layout data, which is significantly less compared to what image models are trained on. A major direction in video-based scene generation involves camera-controlled models. For example, Stable Virtual Camera demonstrated scene navigation and traversal through fine-tuning video diffusion models, enabling camera-controlled multi-view generation. This results in compelling novel-view synthesis that can later be used for further three-dimensional reconstruction. Expanding on this technique, WorldExplorer has taken it further to generate large three-dimensional scenes that can be reconstructed in three dimensions and arbitrarily rendered from new perspectives.

Unlike these approaches, the authors use VLM-based agents to guide the process and verify the generated frames without needing a manually crafted trajectory generation process. They do not use any fine-tuned camera-controlled models, relying instead on existing text- and image-to-image two-dimensional foundation models.

Moving on to the subsection on Two-Dimensional Foundation Image Models. The past years have seen unprecedented advances in two-dimensional image generation models. Recent models can be conditioned on both text and multiple images at the same time to achieve text-conditioned editing capabilities. Various methods have built upon these models to explore capabilities for other downstream tasks such as three-dimensional reconstruction using score distillation sampling and personalization. Such image foundation models show strong capabilities in those downstream tasks. Their powerful generative and perception capacities have also inspired our approach to leveraging image foundation models to their full extent to understand if they would generate three-dimensionally consistent views. NanoBanana and Flux.2 are some of the most recent methods that can generate high-fidelity images within a few seconds. We aim to exploit the full power of those image synthesis models to generate traversable three-dimensional scenes.

Recently, agent-based methods have achieved remarkable success across various domains. These approaches leverage the robust visual and textual reasoning capabilities of Vision-Language Model agents to tackle diverse tasks. Closest to the authors' work is VIGA, which translates images into three-dimensional scenes by generating corresponding Blender code. Their experiments demonstrate that Vision-Language Models possess a deep semantic understanding of scenes and can effectively manipulate code representations for image-to-3D reconstruction.

Inspired by the strong reasoning capabilities of Vision-Language Models and recent advancements in two-dimensional foundation models, the authors introduce a method for frame-by-frame 3D world generation. Unlike VIGA, which relies on a proxy code representation for static 3D reconstruction, their method directly generates image frames, and the ultimate objective is the synthesis of interactive, navigable three-dimensional worlds from text prompts.

In this section, we delve into the method proposed by the authors for 3D scene generation, which is depicted in Figure 1. The process is formulated as a collaborative effort among three specialized agents: a Generator, a Verifier, and a Director.

The Generator operates as a two-dimensional image foundation model. It is capable of synthesizing images conditioned on both textual and visual input. This model is employed to inpaint specific regions of an image, guided by scene captions that are generated by the Director.

To ensure global consistency, the Verifier plays a crucial role. It evaluates each newly generated image by comparing it against a history of previously accepted views. In doing so, the Verifier maintains an intermediate three-dimensional reconstruction to ensure that the generated views collectively form a geometrically coherent 3D space.

The overall iterative process is guided by the Director. This agent analyzes the history of verified views to propose descriptive prompts, which are used to generate novel viewpoints. When the Director assesses that the scene is comprehensively covered, the generation process concludes. Subsequently, the accumulated views are used to reconstruct the final three-dimensional Gaussian Splatting representation using a tool named AnySplat.

Figure 1, titled "Method overview," illustrates this multi-agent approach. It shows how the Director, Generator, and Verifier collaborate to construct coherent 3D scenes utilizing image diffusion models. The Director orchestrates the entire process by creating novel prompts. The Generator then uses sequential inpainting to synthesize views that are consistent across three dimensions. Following this, the Verifier assesses these generated views to ensure they maintain strict multi-view consistency. The verified frames are ultimately reconstructed into a 3D Gaussian Splatting representation.

The problem formulation begins with the goal of converting a given input text description, labeled as y sub one, into a spatially coherent three-dimensional scene denoted by script W. This scene should be representative of the input text y sub one. More specifically, the authors aim to produce a set of N posed images that, when combined, define a three-dimensional world. These images can then be reconstructed as three-dimensional Gaussians to facilitate navigation and exploration within this world. The agents involved in this process are given a total of R-hat number of tries to generate these N images. Alongside each image, the text description for each frame is included in the world state. Overall, the world state consists of a series of verified two-dimensional images, their respective camera poses, and corresponding text prompts, all acquired through an agentic process utilizing two-dimensional foundation models and visual language models.

Formally, the scene is represented by the set script W equals the collection of ordered triples: I sub i, P sub i, and y sub i, for i from 1 to N. Here, each I sub i represents a high-fidelity image, while P sub i is its corresponding absolute camera pose within the global coordinate system. It is important to note that the initial frame generation step is somewhat unique, as it does not involve a director agent. This initial step is solely a text-to-image generation task using y sub one.

The authors describe the generation of a three-dimensional world as an iterative, agent-directed process. At each discrete time step, which they denote as t, a director agent, referred to as script D, analyzes the current world state, denoted by W sub t, being the set of images, poses, and text prompts from 1 to t. This agent proposes how to expand the region by generating a text prompt y sub t. When the overall generation process reaches half of R-hat tries, the process shifts to exploring the left direction. The next camera view is calculated using the formula P sub t plus one equals P sub t composed with delta P sub t, where delta P sub t represents a relative transformation of the camera towards either the right or left direction by a fixed amount. This transformation also includes a random perturbation to ensure more diverse coverage for the next view.

With the previous view denoted as I sub t and the new camera pose P sub t plus one, a generator agent called script G utilizes a two-dimensional foundation model to synthesize a candidate view, referred to as I-hat sub t plus one. Since two-dimensional foundation models can sometimes suffer from structural hallucinations that violate multi-view geometry constraints, the authors introduce a strict two-stage verifier, referred to as script V. This verifier acts as a binary gating function, evaluating the candidate view I-hat sub t plus one against the established world W sub t across both two-dimensional semantic space and three-dimensional reconstruction space.

The verification function is expressed as V of I-hat sub t plus one and W sub t equals: it returns 1 if the candidate view is semantically and geometrically consistent with the established world W sub t, and 0 otherwise. The candidate view is appended to the global state, which is W sub t plus one, being W sub t union with the set containing the candidate view, pose, and text prompt, only if the verification function returns 1. If the candidate is rejected, it is discarded, and the generation step is re-sampled. By optimizing this discrete acceptance criterion, the authors ensure that the final generated world, script W, adheres to multi-view constraints while leveraging the high visual fidelity of the underlying two-dimensional foundation model.

The entire process concludes when either the maximum number of images, R-hat, is reached or the director agent determines that the entire scene has been observed and issues a stop signal.

The Director agent, referred to as script D, serves as the semantic orchestrator of the three-dimensional world synthesis process. In order to prevent semantic drift and the unconstrained wandering typical of autoregressive video generation, script D dynamically computes the next logical viewpoint based on the exploration history.

At each time step, t, the Director observes the current state of the generated world, denoted as script W sub t, alongside the overarching global text prompt denoted as y. It is parameterized by a Vision-Language Model, or VLM, that acts as a policy. This model maps the environmental context to a view-specific text prompt, labeled as y sub t, by checking out the world state and the previous prompts. Thus, y-sub-t-plus-one equals script D of open parenthesis script W sub t close parenthesis.

Concurrently, y sub t plus one explicitly defines the expected visual content from the new perspective, providing strict semantic conditioning for the Generator. This includes a textual description of where to investigate and what to include in that part of the scene when the camera pose changes. The initial text prompt, y sub 1, is already provided as an input; therefore, the Director agent is not involved in the generation of the first frame.

By prompting the Vision-Language Model to iteratively predict y sub t plus one, the framework functions as an autonomous, context-aware semantic operator. This ensures that the exploration trajectory creates meaningful scenes strictly aligned with the global semantic prior y. For instance, in a sci-fi scene, our Director agent suggests prompts such as "expand further right, seamlessly continuing the sleek metallic wall panels wrapping blue and cyan neon strips, a large translucent cylindrical containment unit with softly pulsing blue lights, and embed a recessed digital control panel." It provides comprehensive and semantically-rich prompts for the next view, while also keeping the overall context of the sci-fi scene in mind.

The trajectory procedure begins from the first frame and initially moves in the right direction, then shifts to the left direction. We prompt the Director about which direction we are heading. After that, we apply a fixed rotation of phi degrees around the up-axis to form what is referred to as R sub fixed. To increase coverage diversity, a random transformation denoted as T sub random is applied on top of that.

The formula used is: P sub t plus one equals T sub random multiplied by R sub fixed multiplied by P sub t. Here, the notation dot indicates matrix multiplication. The change in P time, or delta P sub t, equals T sub random multiplied by R sub fixed. If this process has gone through half of the tries denoted as hat R over 2, the Director switches the exploration to move left of the initial frame.

The Generator agent, referred to as script G, has the task of synthesizing a high-fidelity candidate view, represented as I hat sub t plus 1. This view must adhere to the semantic conditioning y sub t plus 1 provided by the Director and follow the geometric transformation denoted as P sub t plus 1. To incorporate three-dimensional structure and camera awareness into the two-dimensional generation process, the authors reinterpret the two-dimensional generative model as a sequential inpainting process. Each new image that is generated is conditioned on re-rendered views, which are based on the reconstruction from previously generated views, thus ensuring geometric consistency across the scene.

To generate a new image, the researchers first collect script W sub t to reconstruct a 3DGS scene. They then re-render the scene from a new viewpoint to provide it as input to their image diffusion model. Specifically, they utilize AnySplat to elevate script W sub t into a global set of three-dimensional Gaussians, denoted as Theta sub t. This conversion can be expressed as Theta sub t equals the function of AnySplat applied to script W sub t.

Moving on to explore the environment further and to synthesize the subsequent novel view, a target camera pose, P sub t plus 1, is computed. This pose involves a fixed rotation, directing either to the left or right. Instead of relying on a strictly deterministic trajectory, the researchers introduce a stochastic exploration mechanism. This involves applying a randomly perturbed transformation, enabling the generator to achieve a more diverse coverage of the scene. Finally, they employ a Gaussian rasterizer, script R, to render the reconstructed scene from the new viewpoint, P sub t plus 1. This process yields the rendered image, denoted as I superscript warp sub t plus 1. Mathematically, this is expressed as I superscript warp sub t plus 1 equals script R applied to Theta sub t and P sub t plus 1.

As a consequence of camera translation and rotation, I superscript warp sub t plus 1 inevitably contains missing regions due to disocclusions and the new camera field of view. To address this, the authors leverage a pre-trained two-dimensional foundation model, referred to as script G sub inpaint, to complete the missing visual information. The model is conditioned on the known warped pixels and is also influenced by the localized text prompt y sub t from the Director. The expression for this is I hat sub t plus 1 equals script G sub inpaint applied to I superscript warp sub t plus 1 and y sub t plus 1.

By grounding the generation process in explicit three-dimensional reprojection before applying the two-dimensional generative prior, the Generator ensures that the overlapping regions between I sub t and I hat sub t plus 1 remain geometrically rigidly aligned. At the same time, the foundation model is constrained purely to filling in the structurally logical, disoccluded regions.

The authors introduce a rigorous two-stage verifier agent, denoted as script V, to address the problem of structural hallucinations and perspective distortions often found in two-dimensional foundation models. This verifier acts as a definitive gating mechanism to determine which images should compose the three-dimensional world. The verifier ensures that the candidate view, denoted as I-hat sub t plus one, is both semantically aligned with the director's intent and strictly geometrically consistent with the established three-dimensional world, denoted as script V sub t. The verification process is divided into a two-dimensional semantic check and a three-dimensional reconstruction-space check.

First, let's discuss the image-space verification. A vision-language model, or VLM, is employed to assess the semantic coherence and visual quality of the candidate image. This model, referred to as script V sub two D, takes the candidate view I-hat sub t plus one, the world state script W sub t, and the director's prompt y sub t to detect any obvious visual artifacts, domain shifts, or prompt misalignment. The output is a binary decision, denoted as v sub two D, which is either zero or one.

Moving on to the three-dimensional reconstruction-space verification, even if a candidate frame is semantically plausible in two dimensions, it may still contain subtle geometric distortions that violate multi-view consistency. To ensure strict global three-dimensional consistency, the authors assess how the introduction of the candidate view I-hat sub t plus one impacts the overall integrity of the three-dimensional reconstruction of the scene. They define a provisional global state, script W prime sub t plus one, as the union of script W sub t and the set containing I-hat sub t plus one and P-hat sub t plus one. This provisional set represents all verified frames up to step t plus the new candidate. Using a technique called AnySplat, the authors lift this provisional set into a unified three-dimensional representation, yielding a candidate three-dimensional global structure model, denoted as Theta prime sub t plus one.

To quantify the global structural integrity, they render the scene from all historical camera poses, P sub i, ranging from P sub one to P sub t plus one, to obtain a set of reconstructed views, denoted as I superscript render sub one through I superscript render sub t plus one. They then compute standard novel view synthesis metrics: Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio, or PSNR, Structural Similarity Index, or SSIM, and Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity, or LPIPS. These metrics are calculated between every input frame, denoted as I sub i, where I sub t plus one equals I-hat sub t plus one, and its corresponding rendered view I superscript render sub i. 

An inconsistent candidate view will force the AnySplat model to compromise its geometry, resulting in a noticeable degradation in the reconstruction quality of the previously verified frames. The metrics are aggregated into a global quality profile, denoted as S sub global, consisting of the metric sets s superscript i sub metrics for each i from one to t plus one.

Rather than relying solely on rigid, empirically defined thresholds, the authors pass these quantitative scores, along with the visual image pairs I sub i and I superscript render sub i for each i, to a dedicated verifier vision-language model, denoted as script V sub three D. This multimodal agent evaluates the global geometric stability holistically to determine if the candidate view maintains the structural integrity of the three-dimensional world. The final decision is given by v sub three D.

The final acceptance decision is the logical conjunction of both verification steps. This decision is expressed as script V of I-hat sub t plus one equals v sub two D and v sub three D. If the result is one, the candidate is accepted into the global state, updating the verified history to script V sub t plus one, which equals script V sub t united with the set containing I-hat sub t plus one. If the result is zero, the frame is discarded, and the generator is prompted to resample a new candidate. This closed-loop evaluation ensures that the final synthesized world strictly adheres to multi-view geometry without sacrificing the high visual fidelity of the two-dimensional foundation model. If the generator fails to produce a successfully verified image after r-hat tries, both the director output y sub t plus one and P sub t plus one are recalculated to give the model another opportunity to explore.

In this section, we delve into the experiments conducted to evaluate our method for 3D world generation. The analysis includes various two-dimensional image generative models, as well as VLMs. Specifically, the generative models considered are Flux 2 and the Gemini models, while the VLMs include Qwen and GPT models.

The comparison extends to state-of-the-art three-dimensional generative methods, namely Text2Room and WorldExplorer. Text2Room relies on image generative models, while WorldExplorer depends on video generative techniques. To numerically evaluate all these methods, we used the CLIP Score, Inception Score, and CLIP-IQA metrics, all with novel view renderings.

We employed various two-dimensional foundation models and VLMs for our agents. For the former, the models included Flux 2 Klein 9B, Flux 2 Pro, and Nano Banana version 1, also known as Gemini 2.5 Flash. Now, examining the impact of the Verifier Agent, it is clear that the verifier agent mitigates the risk of incorporating geometrically inconsistent views that could otherwise degrade the 3D reconstruction. Image diffusion models may sometimes create artifacts or generate structurally incompatible layouts. If these anomalies are incorporated into the pipeline, they cause irreversible corruption to the global geometry. To address this, the verifier autonomously filters erroneous generations. As demonstrated in the table, the inclusion of this module effectively removes catastrophic failures, leading to an improvement in reconstruction quality. As shown in the figure, it reduces significant blur by avoiding inconsistent frames.

Regarding how much the Director agent contributes to the overall process, naively prompting the image diffusion model for iterative frame generation relies on static scene descriptions coupled with random camera poses, such as "left" or "right." This method often results in semantic redundancy and object duplication. For instance, continually conditioning the generator on the same prompt biases the model to hallucinate duplicate objects in the views rather than exploring new contextual elements. This limits the diversity of objects in the scene. To solve this challenge, the authors introduce the director agent, leveraging a Vision-Language Model, or VLM, to dynamically generate context-aware, explorative prompts conditioned on previously verified views. According to the table, this VLM-based prompting strategy significantly enhances completeness, structural diversity, and the overall fidelity of the generated 3D environments.

Concerning the question of whether sequential inpainting helps with increasing generation quality, it is noted that relying solely on unconstrained image generation does not strictly enforce multi-view consistency. This is true even when the model is conditioned on previous frames and precise textual prompts. To explicitly anchor novel views to the existing geometry, the method utilizes a 3D-aware inpainting step. Specifically, they render an intermediate 3D representation from a novel, unexplored camera pose and instruct the diffusion model to inpaint the unobserved regions. This process is guided by the director agent's localized prompt. Omitting this inpainting formulation results in degraded prompt alignment and scene coherence, as highlighted in the table. This highlights the importance of inpainting for maintaining rigorous 3D consistency throughout the generation process. Additionally, the figure shows that they eventually achieve complete scenes.

Finally, discussing the limitations of their work, the authors employ VLMs as high-level directors and evaluators to distill 3D-consistent, navigable environments from 2D image diffusion models. A highly promising direction for future research is extending this VLM-guided method to existing video diffusion models. In this paper, we investigated the fundamental question of whether two-dimensional foundation image models inherently possess three-dimensional world model capabilities. To explore this, we introduced a novel multi-agent framework comprising a VLM-based director, an image generator, and a two-step verifier that operates across both two-dimensional and three-dimensional spaces. Our extensive evaluations demonstrate that by carefully framing the generation process, we can successfully extract implicit three-dimensional knowledge from these two-dimensional models. Our agentic approach yields expansive, robust, and three-dimensional consistent scene reconstructions that support novel view rendering. Our findings confirm that two-dimensional foundation models do encapsulate a grasp of three-dimensional worlds. Future work can explore extending this multi-agent framework to video diffusion models or dynamic four-dimensional scene generation for interactive world synthesis.

In this section, the authors discuss the system prompts used for their agents. These prompts can be found in Listings referenced as the director system prompt, the verifier system prompt, and the reconstructor system prompt.

The generator agent does not have a dedicated system prompt. This is because it functions as a two-dimensional image generation model. Instead, the directives issued by the director agent are comprehensive enough to provide all necessary details and constraints needed for the generator.

The design of these prompts aims to establish a cohesive agentic framework. By modularizing the overall method, the authors ensure that each agent is assigned specific, well-defined objectives. This approach helps minimize any ambiguity in task assignments.

The first listing is the system prompt for the Director Agent. This prompt is provided to the Vision-Language Model, or VLM, to set up the operational constraints and overarching goals. It instructs the agent on orchestrating the entire generation process.

The next listing is the system prompt for the 2D Verifier Agent. The verifier agent includes a dedicated two-dimensional verification component. This prompt guides the agent to define its task objectives, expected behavior, and the required formats for input and output.
