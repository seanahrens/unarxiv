Democratic AI is Possible. The Democracy Levels Framework Shows How It Might Work.

By Aviv Ovadya, Kyle Redman, Luke Thorburn, and 11 more authors.

Published on November 14, 2024.

This position paper argues that effectively democratizing AI requires democratic governance and alignment of AI, and that this is particularly valuable for decisions with systemic societal impacts. Initial steps, such as Meta's Community Forums and Anthropic's Collective Constitutional AI, have illustrated a promising direction, where democratic processes could be used to meaningfully improve public involvement and trust in critical decisions. To more concretely explore what increasingly democratic AI might look like, we provide a "Democracy Levels" framework and associated tools that: 

First, define milestones toward meaningfully democratic AI, which is also crucial for substantively pluralistic, human-centered, participatory, and public-interest AI. Second, these tools can help guide organizations seeking to increase the legitimacy of their decisions on difficult AI governance and alignment questions. And third, they support the evaluation of such efforts. This framework will continue to be evolved by the AI and Democracy Foundation, AIDF, and associated working groups at democracylevels.org.

Who should steer the development of AI? This question, and similar ones, have emerged with previous technological advances. Existing institutions and power structures will clearly play a significant role in adjudicating these questions. However, AI's general-purpose nature, rapid pace of change, market incentives, geopolitical incentives, and jurisdictional arbitrage opportunities present unprecedented challenges.

Thankfully, recent innovations in collective decision-making point towards a new generation of processes, infrastructures, and institutions to navigate these challenges. These innovations offer new ways to ensure that the development of AI remains human-centered. This means not only at an individual human level but also societally and even globally. These efforts should not be limited to individual governments but should include democratic processes commissioned by corporations, governments, and multilateral institutions. Building on such democratic innovations, our position is that democratic AI is possible and valuable for navigating the systemic societal impacts of AI.

To illustrate more concretely what democratizing AI might look like, we provide the "Democracy Levels" framework. This framework articulates milestones for democratic AI, pluralistic AI, participatory AI, and public AI ecosystems. These ecosystems represent a rapidly evolving set of organizations, institutions, and initiatives focused on ensuring that we have the necessary democratic infrastructure for transitioning to a world with highly-capable AI systems. The framework can help guide organizations and institutions in increasing the legitimacy of difficult AI governance and alignment decisions. It also supports evaluation to identify opportunities for improvement of democratic systems and keep AI organizations accountable.

The Democracy Levels Framework we introduce can be used to evaluate two key aspects: the degree to which democratic systems are used for decision-making, and the quality of those democratic systems and the infrastructure supporting them.

This is designed to be useful both internally to organizations making decisions about AI and externally to those supporting this work and providing accountability.

Moving on to the section titled "Why Democratic AI?"

Democracy and democratization are loaded terms, including in AI. We use democracy here to refer to processes and systems for collective decision-making that are characterized by a kind of equality among the participants at an essential stage of the decision-making process. This reference is made while acknowledging that democracy is a bundle of thick concepts that often include human rights, rule of law, institutional checks and balances, appropriate norms, sanctions, and other features that support such equality. Democratic AI then refers to AI systems whose development, alignment, and governance were substantively influenced or directly controlled by democratic systems. We emphasize that this use of democracy differs from how democratization is often used in the context of AI, where it is often understood as simply making AI open and accessible.

Moving on to the societal challenge.

Machine learning research is progressing at an unprecedented rate. Artificial intelligence systems now make explicit and implicit decisions affecting billions globally, from financial risk assessments to recommender systems. We are increasingly subject to the decisions of, and interactions between, AI agents. Institutional entities and nation-states are rapidly developing AI systems designed to supplant human capabilities while amassing compute and energy infrastructure. This technological trajectory introduces several concerning possibilities: significant power concentration, difficult-to-govern proliferation of advanced capabilities to malicious actors, or gradual disempowerment of most people. AI systems and those that control them may accrue unprecedented power. Who should wield that power? How should it be wielded?

We face many consequential decisions about societal adaptation to AI and significant changes to the dynamics and distribution of power. In this context, democracy can be viewed as a social technology for responsibly making such decisions and managing such power shifts. More precisely, democracy is commonly justified as a way to legitimize the use of power, such as through open and free deliberation, collective self-government, and public justification. It serves to distribute power in ways that reflect intrinsic moral equality and prevent domination. Moreover, democracy helps achieve epistemic benefits that are more difficult when power is concentrated in the hands of a few, rather than many.

The legitimacy and buy-in that democratic processes can generate are increasingly valuable to unilateral actors involved in the development and governance of AI at corporations, governments, and multilateral NGOs. For corporations in particular, we argue that, in addition to the benefits already listed, the costs of using democratic systems for several critical areas of decision-making are substantially lower than reactive compliance with regulation, forced reorganization under antitrust action, or loss of market value due to eroded public trust. Moreover, such democratic systems can help address core organizational barriers such as a bias for inaction due to conflicting internal or external stakeholders. These processes can also help handle controversial decisions, such as those related to sensitive cultural issues, trade-offs between liberty and safety, or influential default behaviors of AI systems.

Finally, we believe that democracy is an asymmetric enabler — that on the margin, greater adoption of democratic processes and systems would relatively advantage good actors and outcomes over bad. In particular, democratic processes can facilitate a number of political moves that may help mitigate risks of race dynamics and unstable multipolar regimes that could plausibly arise as AI technology advances. For example, democratic processes may help signal benign intent, demonstrate Pareto optimality, surface common ground, institutionalize conflict, provide stability by guarantees on process, and be a source of accountability by showing a difference between public will and unilateral actions.

Moving on to discussing how democratic AI can work.

What could AI democratization look like? There are multiple possible approaches. However, pragmatically, the examples we discuss predominantly draw on contemporary deliberative democratic processes. This is due to their potential to simultaneously work with jurisdictions of arbitrary size, including those with less identity infrastructure, and globally. They also complement a wide variety of existing political structures, enable informed exchange incorporating diverse perspectives and expertise, and generatively identify common ground around charged issues.

A democratic process of this form has as its input a remit and constituent population, and as its output a decision. The remit is a prompt that scopes the decision that needs to be made and may specify the structure and properties required for the output. At an essential stage in the process, decisions are made by a representative subset of the constituent population, generally selected by sortition. Processes are often conducted by a third-party democratic infrastructure provider, roughly analogous to an election system vendor, who has expertise in conducting such deliberative processes.

There are already early examples of democratic AI experimentation, including in companies. Most leading AI organizations have begun experimenting with democratic processes for policy or alignment decisions, including Anthropic's Collective Constitutional AI, OpenAI's Democratic Inputs to AI grant program, Meta's Community Forums, and Google DeepMind's STELA project. There is increasing pressure to use such processes for the development of international regulation. Although these initial efforts are imperfect, they are helping build the organizational knowledge and capacity needed for more transformative outcomes. But to fulfill the potential of these processes and ensure they are credible, we need a shared language to describe and evaluate progress, which we will discuss in Sections referred to as framework and application.

Moving on to the Democracy Levels Framework. Effective democratic AI requires compatibility between three key elements. The first is the systems in place for democratic decision-making. The second is the level of power granted to those systems. The third is the context in which decisions are made.

In this section, we introduce the core of the Democracy Levels Framework, which consists of two main components. First, a set of Levels captures the degree to which decision-making power has been transferred to a democratic system. Second, a set of dimensions captures the qualities of that democratic system. Further, section three builds on this with two additional components. A Levels Decision Tool helps in informing decisions about how much democracy is appropriate in a given decision-making context. Also, a Democratic System Card can be used to evaluate a democratic system against the dimensions mentioned earlier.

Moving on to the terminology section. 

We define the term "Scope of Authority" as the set of powers that an authority is granted, including a specification of the domains governed by that authority, and potentially implicit external constraints. For example, the scope of authority of a finance committee for a United States corporation's board of directors is set out by a corporate charter, bylaws, and board resolutions. It is constrained by regulations and case law, and can have its authority limited to financial matters. 

We then move on to the term "Unilateral authority," which is defined as an entity that can make decisions without meaningful checks on its power within a given scope of authority. In other words, no approval is needed for any decisions within its scope.

Next, we define "Democratic Process" as a collective decision-making process characterized by a kind of equality among the participants at an essential stage of the decision-making process.

Furthermore, we define "Democratic System" as a set of interacting entities and democratic processes, such as through checks and balances.

Under these definitions, elections, citizen assemblies, and collective dialogue processes are considered democratic processes. The interactions between those processes and unilateral authorities, constituents, stakeholders, media, and so on make up a democratic system that is supported via democratic infrastructure. 

A more complex democratic system might involve an entire constitutional order with multiple institutions interacting through different processes on an ongoing basis. For example, a repeated collective alignment process, feeding into a model specification, managed by a democratic oversight body, all coordinated by democratic infrastructure providers, could have a scope of authority over AI model alignment.

Moving on to the levels of democratic decision-making. The transfer of decision-making power from a unilateral authority to democratic systems can take many forms. However, there are discrete points of particular significance that may require new democratic infrastructure and other structural changes. We developed the Democracy Levels to provide clearer distinctions for understanding and implementing these transfers, building on experience supporting movement between these levels.

We define each level of democratic decision-making according to which of five roles is performed by democratic systems, rather than a unilateral authority. These roles include: informing decisions, specifying decisions, making binding decisions, automatically triggered initiation of binding decision-making processes, and metagovernance. Figure 1 provides definitions for each level, from L0 to L5, along with concrete examples of what this could look like in practice for a plausible decision domain, such as developing a set of rules governing persuasion by an AI system. Such rules might be used directly in model training, for example, to align an AI system, or as policies for an AI organization or regulator.

At level 1, the democratic authority only provides input, which is interpreted as the unilateral authority wishes, and thus can be in any form. At level 2, the decisions need to be directly implementable, such as a policy or model specification, but may be vetoed by a predetermined process. From level 3 onward, all decisions are binding within their scope of authority. Level 4 involves a democratic system that automatically initiates level 3 binding processes when particular conditions are met. Finally, at level 5, the democratic system also does metagovernance within its scope of authority. This could involve making decisions on how to run level 4 processes, potentially via a set of checks and balances across different bodies and processes.

This framework particularly takes inspiration from the autonomy levels defined for self-driving cars, which also involve the shifting of power and responsibility from a unilateral authority, such as a human driver or AI corporation, to a new kind of decision-making system, like an autonomous control system or democratic system.

A single AI organization, government, or AI system may simultaneously implement multiple democratic systems at different levels for different decision domains. For example, an AI organization's decisions about whether to release a new model might be at level 2, while decisions about the model specification used for fine-tuning could be at level 4. There is also flexibility on the scope of authority for a process. For example, it is possible to have a level 3 process with the scope of authority specified as binding for two years, or until a given condition is met, such as a model passing a particular benchmark.

Moving on to the section on dimensions.

While the levels specify which roles are performed by democratic systems as opposed to the unilateral authority, the dimensions, as seen in Table one, convey the extent to which democratic systems are "good enough" to support the meaningful, safe, and effective implementation of higher democracy levels.

In some cases, if a democratic system is deficient in some way, such as leaving participants uninformed, lacking representativeness, or not being robust to adversaries, blindly moving to higher democracy levels can be risky since it could result in binding to a poorly made decision. The assessment of dimensions is also context-sensitive. Whether a democratic system is "good enough" can depend on specific contextual factors like the public's trust, understanding of the process, and willingness to participate, and so on.

Below, we describe three primary dimension categories: process quality, delegation, and trust, each with several dimensions.

For a democratic system or infrastructure component to be able to support more decision-making responsibility, it must be able to reliably provide a certain level of process quality. More concretely, process quality dimensions evaluate whether the decisions produced are representative of the relevant population, participants are informed on the issue, processes involve deliberative reasoning, decision outputs are substantive, systems are robust to adversarial behavior and less-than-ideal conditions, and the final traces are legible, meaning transparent and understandable to non-participants.

Additionally, the unilateral authority needs to be able to effectively delegate to the democratic system. This includes the capacity of the unilateral authority to organizationally and publicly commit to the outcomes, to integrate such processes into its operations, and to technically and/or legally bind itself to the resulting decisions.

Finally, to ensure the process decision is accepted, there must be external conditions that support the success of the process, which we collectively refer to as trust. Specifically, the relevant public and stakeholders must be sufficiently aware of the process, buy into its legitimacy, be willing to participate, and there must be sufficiently capable forms of accountability.

To further illustrate our framework, we can apply the levels to discuss the transition of decision-making power in the wild. For example, Anthropic's Collective Constitutional AI effort involved a roughly representative microcosm of the United States public providing and evaluating principles for an AI system to follow. These principles were de-duplicated and slightly transformed for training a research model, with one of the clauses used for training a deployed model. This process informed model development, so it can arguably be seen as an example of a transition from level zero, unilateral, to level one, informing decisions. Had Anthropic predetermined a process or criteria for de-duplicating, transforming, and ultimately accepting or rejecting the output of the process, this could have been an example of a level two process. With a binding commitment to adopt, the same process could even be brought up to level three.

Another example can be found in Meta's Oversight Board's content moderation decisions about individual posts, which corresponds to a transfer of decision-making power at level four, regular binding decisions. The Oversight Board's broader policy advisory opinions are non-binding and thus only operate at level one. All that said, evaluating the dimensions, we can observe that the Oversight Board was not designed to be democratically representative. In contrast, Meta's Community Forums on AI are intended to be representative; however, they only inform policies and product decisions, and so are also level one.

In developing this framework, we have drawn inspiration from various existing frameworks that evaluate democratic-ness. These include works by Arnstein from 1969, Lindberg in 2014, IAP2 Australasia, and Skaaning in 2023. Additionally, we were inspired by frameworks assessing the degrees of responsible behavior and autonomy in AI systems, as explored by Bommasani in 2024 and SAE in 2021. Our work is related to explorations and assessments across several areas, including democratic by the Collective Intelligence Project in 2024, participatory efforts by Delgado in 2023, Cooper in 2024, and Suresh in 2024, pluralistic approaches by Sorensen in 2024, human-centered methods by Sigfrids in 2023, and public AI initiatives by the Public AI Network in 2024 and Vincent in 2023. More details about these efforts surrounding democratic AI are discussed in the Appendix.

In Table 1, we present the dimensions of the Democracy Levels Framework alongside an overview of the Democratic System Card. This table provides a set of questions designed to guide reflection on whether the quality of a democratic system aligns with the level of decision-making power delegated to it. You can see further details, examples, and templates for completing Democratic System Cards in Appendix, as well as on the website democracylevels.org/system-card.

The table is structured to include the dimensions of democracy level with corresponding questions. Under the header "Process Quality," which relates to the democratic infrastructure, several dimensions are provided. The first dimension is Representation. This pertains to the extent to which key decisions represent the constituent population. The guiding questions are: To what extent is there sufficient representation at critical parts of the process, including proposing and making ultimate decisions, and are there barriers leading to bias in representation? 

The next dimension is Informedness. This assesses whether critical information is considered during decision-making. Questions include the extent to which critical context is included in decision-making about tradeoffs and consequences, and whether this information is sourced from domain expertise, existing authorities, a broad diversity of constituents, the most impacted stakeholders, and the powerful stakeholders.

Deliberation is another dimension, focusing on whether decisions are considered and deliberative instead of superficial and reactive. The questions here assess the extent to which those involved are able to move from shallow to deeper goals and values, collaborate where necessary, and address issues within the available time.

Substantiveness evaluates whether decisions are substantive, such as being actionable and consequential, rather than nonsubstantive. Questions explore whether the decision is directly actionable and implementable, meaningfully addresses issues, grapples with necessary complexity, manages uncertainty appropriately, and accounts for risks to implementability.

Robustness looks at whether the process is robust to suboptimal conditions or adversarial or strategic behavior. It questions the extent to which the process or system is vulnerable to suboptimal conditions, broken assumptions, strategic behavior, manipulation, and false claims.

Finally, Legibility relates to the accessibility, understandability, and verifiability of processes and decisions. It considers whether information regarding processes and systems used to make decisions, the execution of these processes, decisions being made, and reasons and inputs feeding into decisions, are accessible, understandable, and verifiable.

Under "Delegation," which relates to unilateral or existing authorities, the dimension of Integration assesses how well the authority integrates the democratic process into its operations. This includes whether it provides critical context to the democratic process, integrates democratic process outputs in its actions, and triggers democratic processes when necessary.

The next dimension, Ability to bind, examines whether the authority can technically and legally bind itself to democratic decisions. This involves whether the authority has developed the necessary technical and legal infrastructure for binding.

Commitment looks at the extent to which the authority commits to acting in accordance with the democratic decision. This dimension assesses whether the authority has committed internally, privately, and publicly, regardless of its ability to bind.

Next, we move on to "Trust," which relates to external conditions. The first dimension is Awareness, exploring whether the relevant public is aware of the democratic process. The guiding questions probe the extent to which the public knows that the democratic system exists, how it works, what it is used for, and how they can be involved.

Participation considers the public's willingness and ability to participate in the democratic process. It questions the extent to which the public is willing, able, appropriately compensated for participating, and actually participating.

Accountability examines whether there are external watchdogs and accountability structures monitoring the execution of the democratic process and the implementation of its outputs. It looks at whether there are well-understood lines of oversight and accountability, sufficiently influential organizations holding authorities to their promised levels of involvement, and whether authorities and systems are responsive to accountability mechanisms.

Finally, Buy-in reflects the public's and key stakeholders' acceptance of the process and its legitimacy. Questions consider to what extent the relevant public and key stakeholders accept the legitimacy of the system and the decisions.

Moving on to the discussion of Democracy Levels Tools. While the main components of levels and dimensions offer a common language to discuss the allocation and transition of decision-making power, in practice, balancing key elements of democratic AI — such as context, level, and system — when planning for transition can be challenging. To make it easier for stakeholders to plan and evaluate possible transitions between levels, we introduce two tools that are meant to help ground the thinking process for various stakeholders.

The first tool, known as the Levels Decision Tool, is intended to support unilateral authorities and advocates in planning out the transition to higher levels of democratic decision-making. The second tool, called the Democratic System Card, is intended to support the assessment of democratic systems. This allows stakeholders to understand their fitness for adoption at a desired level and to identify opportunities for innovation and improvement.

Moving on to the Levels Decision Tool.

The Levels Decision Tool, a version of which is provided in the appendix, was developed to pragmatically help evaluate the potential reasons for delegating a decision or decision-domain to a democratic system. It may be used by unilateral authorities directly for their own decision-making, or by stakeholders and advocates seeking to identify the appropriate arguments needed to demonstrate the value of democratic systems to those authorities.

Which level to aim for depends on the context of the decision, including the unilateral authority, its scope of authority, who is affected by the decision, and how the authority relates to other stakeholders, both internal and external.

The Levels Decision Tool contains a set of targeted questions around this context that can help decision-makers consider which democracy level to aim for. The questions involve the value of legitimacy across the public, internal stakeholders, external stakeholders, and government; the potential benefits of collective intelligence; the feasibility of transferring decision-making power; the importance of speed and adaptability to the decision domain; resourcing; and novelty. Some questions are applicable to every context, and some are only applicable to, for example, corporations.

Moving on to the Democratic System Card.

The Democratic System Card is intended to help decision-makers document, assess, compare, and evaluate democratic systems in a structured manner, with a core goal of providing insight into how appropriate a system is at a given democracy level, for a given context. The system card, referenced in the document and in the appendix, also provides an elaboration of the three dimensions we derived earlier: process quality, delegation, and trust.

A full system card has three primary components. First, there are descriptions of how the democratic system works overall with respect to each dimension. Second, there are assessments of the system implementation with respect to each dimension, based on a series of guiding questions. Finally, there is a qualitative evaluation of the highest level of power that the system can be trusted with for making decisions, tailored to a given context.

In a similar vein to model cards and AI system cards that have been discussed in prior works, democratic system cards support evaluating a democratic system within different contexts. Decision-makers can use system cards to assess whether a democratic system is ready to be delegated to higher levels of decision-making power, with more clarity around which dimensions within the system are the current bottlenecks. Stakeholders and advocacy groups can make use of system cards to compare and contrast possible alternative systems to propose or advocate for. While active and empowered democratic systems, or proposals for complete democratic systems, should have complete cards, prototypes, and research projects may have only parts of the card filled out, as not every aspect is applicable.

Democratic system cards are also meant to be a resource for those identifying gaps in the democratic infrastructure ecosystem, including those exploring potential opportunities and applications of their research. They can be used by process designers and democratic infrastructure providers for understanding critical needs for operating at higher levels of delegated power, and to consider which combinations of democratic processes complement one another.

Moving on to alternative views, we summarize common objections and responses to them using a question-and-answer format. Additional alternative views can be found in the appendix.

First question: Shouldn't people make their own choices about how they use AI? This libertarian critique argues that individuals are the best judges of what is in their interests and should be free to make their own choices, provided that those choices do not harm others.

Answer: We agree, with critical caveats. Ideally, most decisions would be made by individuals or users; however, democratic processes may be needed for addressing systemic impacts, significant externalities, and decisions where revealed preferences diverge significantly from more reflective or deliberative preferences. Even without these conditions, there often remains a significant barrier to individual choice in practice due to the lack of interoperability and the resulting friction in moving between different AI systems, particularly when these systems are directly integrated into devices, products, and services.

Next question: Shouldn't governments just regulate? This government-only critique cites corporations' poor track record of addressing the systemic issues relating to their activities.

Answer: While government action is crucial and the Democracy Levels Framework can be applied to regulators, it is insufficient alone. Given the potentially extraordinary benefits and risks of AI, and the influence of corporations on the trajectory of AI development, a full-stack approach to democratic AI is likely necessary. Organizations developing AI systems are closer to the critical context needed to make informed decisions and can make these decisions more rapidly than governments can respond with regulation. The framework can help to avoid democratic legitimacy relying on the slowest-moving actor in a jurisdiction. 

Moreover, governments themselves may be less democratic, with autocrats and politicians seeking to concentrate and entrench their own power. It may be much faster to innovate in creating fit-for-purpose democratic systems for governing AI by experimenting in industry and then bringing them into government once the systems are more mature. Finally, the organizations developing AI, the use of AI, and the risks of AI can all be transnational, beyond the jurisdiction of any single government.

Moving to the next question: Shouldn't AI corporations focus solely on shareholders and profit? This shareholder-first critique argues that shifting power from shareholders and executives to democratic systems would decrease societal benefit, under the assumption that maximizing corporate profits for shareholders will also maximize societal good.

Answer: Shareholder maximization may be societally beneficial if externalities are sufficiently internalized, but this generally requires effective government action, as noted in the previous answer. The competing Stakeholder Theory aims to address some of these gaps by encouraging corporations to consider stakeholder impacts, and democratic processes are precisely a means for implementation. Furthermore, most shareholder pressure is a slow and blunt instrument, with significant lag; given the pace of AI change, a more responsive form of societal feedback may be critical. Broad-based societal benefits seem especially unlikely if profit-maximizing AI-first corporations continue to increasingly dominate the economy, leaving more and more people outside of both democratic and economic feedback loops.

That said, even under a shareholder primacy model, trust and legitimacy may become increasingly salient for corporate executives and shareholders as the societal importance of AI increases, due to a mix of procurement, regulatory, and universal owner pressures. As the Levels Decision Tool shows, there are numerous potential benefits from devolving some power.

Finally, the question arises: Wouldn't this slow down the development of AI? This accelerationist critique reflects a concern that using democratic processes will stymie AI progress and development, reducing the capabilities of democratic nations compared to their non-democratic counterparts, and negatively impacting valuable innovation and the relative power of democratic nations.

Answer: Democratic systems can be very slow and ineffective, but that is not inevitable, especially with sufficient investment in democratic innovation, such as the development of augmented deliberative democratic processes. Concerns about the relative position of democratic versus non-democratic states are driven, at least in theory, by a desire to protect democratic values. Applying democratic processes to AI governance is a demonstration of exactly those democratic values and their benefits. High-quality decision-making and regulation, stemming from improved democratic processes, may even create conditions that accelerate innovation.

Discussion. Given the increasingly systemic impacts of AI, it seems increasingly plausible that democratic AI will be a necessary, though insufficient, part of any potential positive future. The machine learning community has a significant role to play in enabling that future, both by improving our democratic infrastructure and by helping raise awareness about the importance of connecting technical capabilities to democratic oversight, particularly as AI systems become more powerful.

Moving on to the discussion of what decisions should be made democratically. The kinds of decisions that should be made democratically are contestable. While the Levels Decision Tool, which is described in another section, summarizes relevant considerations, our framework is intentionally agnostic on this question.

However, in broad strokes, we think there is a stronger moral case for a unilateral authority to devolve decision-making power to a democratic process or system when several conditions are met. First, the decision involves externalities, which are costs or benefits to third parties that are not borne by the unilateral authority. Second, those impacted by the decision cannot easily or reasonably opt out of experiencing such impacts. Third, the impacts of the decisions are substantive enough to make up for the costs of the democratic system.

Without intending to be stipulative or exhaustive, we think examples of decisions that may plausibly fit these criteria include several categories. Firstly, governance decisions made by AI regulators that significantly alter societal norms or socioeconomic conditions. Examples include guardrails or limits on human-AI relationships, whether or how AI agents can be legal persons, whether or how AI agents can participate in the economy, and whether or how AI agents can replace human workers.

Secondly, development and deployment decisions made by AI organizations that impact large user bases, have significant cascading impacts, pose significant systemic or societal risks, or significantly accelerate or shift societal trajectories relative to counterfactual alternative decisions. Examples here include decisions around what safety thresholds must be met before the release or deployment of increasingly capable models, decisions to significantly alter the behavior of models to which many people have formed economic or emotional dependencies, and decisions relating to what organizational structures and incentives influential labs subject themselves to.

Lastly, decisions made by AI systems for which there should be a "society-in-the-loop." Examples include decisions made by AI systems that play significant roles in the functioning of utility-scale infrastructure.

Moving on to future directions. There is a vast array of critical work needed to make democratic AI a reality across AI systems, regulators, and organizations. This involves a mix of evaluation, system design, pilots, and institutionalization. As a concrete technical example, social choice analysis can help us understand the extent to which the current machine learning paradigms may already be implicitly democratic or undemocratic.

Advancements in AI may also help improve many kinds of democratic processes and systems, with recent studies demonstrating their potential to mediate human deliberation and find common ground. AI systems have also been used to synthesize diverse viewpoints within large populations, whilst maintaining cultural and contextual nuances, and promote constructive disagreement. Applying these advances to build, test, and validate fit-for-purpose democratic systems can help increase the extent to which such systems are viable alternatives to unilateral decision-making.

To rigorously assess the "democratic-ness" of such AI-enabled democratic systems, research is needed to develop domain-specific metrics and evaluation frameworks. Both approaches, "democracy for AI" and "AI for democracy," would benefit from further research on integration into existing governance and technical structures and processes.

Maturity in the democratic governance of artificial intelligence won't come overnight. Organizations, democratic infrastructure providers, stakeholders, and the public all need to build democratic muscle, and taking on too much all at once can backfire. Instead of holding organizations to a platonic ideal, it can often be more helpful to focus on improvements relative to the status quo or comparable organizations. Both of these can be articulated through a leveling and evaluation system. Spelling out the larger milestones toward achieving an audacious goal can also provide motivation to achieve those goals, and even enable the creation of powerful incentives such as advanced market commitments.

The framework that we provide can be used to build up a democratic capacity and to clarify when organizations are claiming to be acting more democratically than they actually are. This can then provide a basis for ensuring that they live up to their professed standards. Such differentials between ambitious democratic aspirations and reality have been a major force for democracy across history. True democratization is a journey, and we aim to have provided a useful map.

Artificial intelligence is reshaping the world. If we are to preserve liberty and agency over our future, we see few alternatives: we must have democratic systems fit for the age of artificial intelligence. By providing a concrete articulation that may be contested and built upon, we hope that the call for democratic artificial intelligence, and the Democracy Levels Framework, may enable more productive conversations about what futures we should be aiming for with regard to power, participation, pluralism, and democracy.

This supports lower-level planning around the specific democratic system or process to adopt in a transition.

We present the Levels Decision Tool, designed to assist in determining the appropriate Democracy Level to target in a given context. This tool is comprised of a series of questions. Each question is associated with arrows indicating whether an affirmative answer suggests aiming for a higher or lower Democracy Level. In some instances, both directions are suggested, implying that the decision depends on the context.

For the latest version of this tool, including an editable version in Google Docs, please visit democracylevels.org/decision-tool.

In the first section of the tool, titled "General trust and legitimacy," we ask to what extent trust and legitimacy are important to the unilateral authority and whether this authority is trusted by others, irrespective of their decisions. If trust and legitimacy are important or the authority is not trusted, the recommendation leans towards aiming for a higher level.

Further questions assess the decision context, considering whether there is no clear expert consensus, significant public interest or externality concerns, limited public impact, a private technical or operational matter, or again, no clear expert consensus. Here, the direction for each decision may vary, leaning mostly towards higher levels when significant public concerns are involved.

The section "External stakeholder legitimacy" examines the presence of powerful stakeholder groups with conflicting perspectives, a bias for inaction given such conflicts, and opportunities for decreased criticism through process legitimacy. Each of these scenarios suggests aiming for a higher Democracy Level.

Next, "Internal stakeholder legitimacy" explores the extent of significant internal disagreement, the need to evolve organizational values or purposes with a strong mandate, motivation by public benefit, a history of collaborative decision-making, and the presence of a hierarchical structure. Additionally, this section looks at extensive collaboration between teams or departments. While internal disagreement, need for evolving purposes, public benefit motivation, and collaborative history suggest a higher level, hierarchical structures and collaboration complexity offer mixed implications, sometimes reducing respect for democratic systems.

The "Government risk" section addresses the risk of backlash, such as antitrust or regulatory repercussions, and pressure from political figures to act against public wishes. The presence of limited oversight and a complex regulatory landscape also feature here. High scrutiny environments can support democratization for favorable regulation, though a lack of regulator experience might limit influence. Clearly defined laws suggest a lower level.

Under "Collective intelligence," we explore whether the decision benefits from diverse perspectives, locally distributed knowledge, high uncertainty, or involves complex interdependencies. Generally, broad diversity and distributed knowledge support higher levels, whereas complex interdependencies suggest lower ones.

The "Viability" section covers political obstacles to power delegation and addresses whether organizational, legal, technical, or physical obstacles exist. Democratic legitimacy can potentially overcome barriers, but when obstacles persist despite democratic norms, a different approach is warranted.

In the "Resources" section, we evaluate whether resource levels match the significance of decisions and whether recurring expenses can be supported. Adequate resources are crucial for higher levels, particularly levels four and five.

Regarding "Speed," the tool considers time-critical response needs and emergency situations. While time constraints and regular time-critical scenarios suggest processes that support higher levels, emergencies warrant caution for complex system exclusion.

The "Adaptability" section questions the anticipation of rapid changes impacting decisions, their relevance, or their remit. More adaptable systems can counteract such challenges, supporting higher levels.

Lastly, under "Novelty," we examine the novelty of decision devolution. A first-mover advantage in democratic processes can enhance respect and newsworthiness, but it also introduces unknown risks.

This comprehensive tool serves as a guide to navigate the complexities associated with determining the appropriate Democracy Level in various contexts.

The Democratic System Card is a set of questions designed to guide reflection on whether the quality of a democratic system is commensurate with the level of decision-making power delegated to it, for a given domain of decision-making, in a given context. The questions are grouped by the dimensions in the Democracy Levels Framework.

To complete a System Card, you follow several steps. First, describe the process or system at a high level. Then, summarize what other systems the process depends on or interacts with, which impact its success. Examples include sortition data, or user or citizen authentication systems. Next, for each dimension or property defined on the card below, describe how that dimension works in the process or system you are evaluating. Lastly, use the guiding questions to reflect on the extent to which that dimension is satisfied.

You can find the most up-to-date version of the Democratic System Card, including editable templates that can be initially filled with a large language model, given context, at democracylevels.org/system-card.

Moving on to the context, the first task is to describe the process or system at a high level. This can reference a process card for more details and can identify what is unspecified. Next, consider other systems that this process depends on or interacts with, which impact its success. Examples include sortition data, or user or citizen authentication systems.

Next, we examine process quality, starting with representation. Describe the extent to which key decisions are representative of the constituent population. Then evaluate to what extent there is sufficient representation at critical parts of the process, including proposing decisions and making ultimate decisions. Also consider if there are barriers leading to bias in representation.

The next aspect is informedness. Describe the extent to which critical information is taken into account for decision-making. Evaluate to what extent critical context is incorporated into decision-making about tradeoffs and consequences of different decisions. Consider if this information is sourced from domain expertise, existing authorities who may have extensive context, a broad diversity of constituents, the most impacted stakeholders, and the powerful stakeholders, whose incentives are critical to having the decision "stick."

Now, examine deliberation. Describe the extent to which decisions are considered and deliberative rather than superficial and reactive. Evaluate to what extent those involved are able to and supported to move from shallower to deeper goals and values, collaborate where necessary, and address issues within the available time.

The next concept is substantiveness. Describe the extent to which decisions are substantive, meaning they are actionable and consequential, rather than nonsubstantive, such as vague, simplistic, or inconsequential. Evaluate to what extent the decision is directly actionable and implementable, meaningfully addresses the issues, grapples with the necessary levels of complexity, and manages and accounts for uncertainty and risks to implementability.

Now, consider robustness. Describe the extent to which the process is robust to suboptimal conditions or adversarial or strategic behavior. Evaluate to what extent the process or system is vulnerable to suboptimal conditions or broken assumptions, such as low turnout or larger power asymmetries. Also consider vulnerabilities to strategic behavior and manipulation, and false claims such as those of manipulation.

Next is legibility. Describe the extent to which the processes and decisions are accessible, understandable, and verifiable. Evaluate to what extent information is accessible, understandable, and verifiable about the processes or systems used to make decisions, the execution of these processes, the decisions being made, and the reasons and inputs feeding into decisions.

Now, let's discuss delegation, starting with integration. Describe the extent to which the authority integrates the democratic process into its operations. Evaluate to what extent the authority is structuring its internal communications and operations to effectively provide critical context to the democratic process or system, integrate democratic process outputs in its actions, and trigger democratic processes when or if required.

Next is the ability to bind. Describe the extent to which the authority is able to technically and legally bind itself to democratic decisions. Evaluate to what extent the unilateral authority can bind itself to acting in accordance with the democratic decision, both technically and legally, such as having developed the needed technical and or legal infrastructure for binding.

Then, there is commitment. Describe the extent to which the unilateral authority commits to acting in accordance with the democratic decision. Evaluate to what extent the unilateral authority has committed to acting in accordance with the democratic decision internally, privately, and publicly, regardless of their ability to bind.

Moving on to trust, beginning with awareness. Describe the extent to which the relevant public is aware of the democratic process. Evaluate to what extent the relevant public is aware that the democratic system exists, how it works, what it is being used for, and how they can be involved.

Next is participation. Describe the extent to which the relevant public is willing to participate in the process. Evaluate to what extent the relevant public is willing to participate, able to participate, appropriately compensated for participating, and actually participating.

The last aspect in trust is accountability. Describe the extent to which there are external watchdogs and accountability structures monitoring the execution of the democratic process and the implementation of its outputs. Evaluate to what extent there are well-understood lines of oversight and accountability, sufficiently influential or powerful organizations focused on holding authorities to their promised levels of democratic involvement, and whether authorities and democratic systems are responsive to such accountability mechanisms.

Finally, we consider buy-in. Describe the extent to which the relevant public and key stakeholders buy into the process and its legitimacy. Evaluate to what extent the relevant public and key stakeholders are accepting of the legitimacy of the system or process and of the decision.

Moving on to the design decisions section.

There are a few notable decisions embedded in this framework. We intentionally divorce process quality from delegation, as third-party democratic infrastructure providers can be commissioned by unilateral authorities such as AI labs and regulators. This is referenced in the works of Chang in 2024, Ovadya in 2023, Broxmeyer in 2024, as well as RSA in 2019, and Anthropic in 2023. This separation of concerns can help prevent fraud and provide an opportunity for process improvements by one organization to be passed on to other organizations. Operationally, at level one, such commissioned deliberations are roughly analogous to commissioning representative surveys or community engagement processes. Above that level, the processes being commissioned are more sophisticated and more directly integrated into organizational decision-making.

These dimensions also have significant dependencies. For example, processes that fail to demonstrate sufficient process quality given the level of power entrusted to them, or delegation, are likely to lead to backlash or low trust. This can happen if a process is subverted, or if a democratic decision sounds good but ends up being counterproductive.

Most, if not all, of these dimensions have long been studied in democratic theory and related disciplines and can be operationalized in different ways. For example, see the many conceptions of representation, as noted by Dovi in 2018. For now, we aim for the framework to be agnostic to these differences.

Moving on to more alternative views. A question often asked is, why should non-experts make decisions on technical matters? This technocratic critique is concerned with poor decisions being made by non-experts in a democratic process because AI is highly complex and can be conceptually difficult to understand.

Our answer is that we agree non-experts should not be making technical decisions, in the same way that no one wants a non-medical professional to diagnose them. However, many of the decisions about AI, from which data to collect to goal specification, are or can be highly value-laden. The assumption that such decisions are too complex is also debatable, given that there have been successful democratic processes, such as Citizens' Assemblies, that have involved non-expert members of the public to produce informed decisions on problems of similar complexity. These processes have mechanisms to deal with the expertise gap via educational components, expert consultation, and similar means.

The next question asks, why would people want to take part in the democratic governance of AI? The argument at the heart of this busy lives critique is that people are busy and so will not want to dedicate time to a task that they may see as a job for corporations and perhaps the government.

In response, we accept that not everyone will have the time or the interest to participate in the democratic governance of AI. However, the Democracy Levels Framework is compatible with a range of democratic processes. It is not the case that everyone needs to be interested all of the time for the democratic governance of AI to be viable.

Another common critique is that democracy isn't just collective decision-making or deliberation—what about other important elements or theories of democracy? Some of these democratic critiques argue that there are other fundamental elements to democracy beyond mere preference aggregation, including respect for human rights, rule of law, equality before the law, and freedom of expression. Others might argue that the framework is tailored too closely to participatory ideals of democracy, and is less accommodating of, for example, representative or agonistic theories of what democracy is or should be.

Our answer is that we agree democracy is more than collective decision-making, especially when implemented at the level of nation-states, but there are already frameworks for evaluating democracy in such settings. The core of our framework, the Levels, focuses strategically on the transfer of decision-making power from unilateral authorities to relevant communities because we believe it to be a necessary, if not always sufficient, element of democratic-ness. This can be evaluated not just for governments and regulators but also for organizations and AI models. The framework is also agnostic with respect to the preferred theory of democracy. All theories describe some formal or informal process by which collective decisions are made, so they can be evaluated against the Levels, and the desiderata described in the dimensions and Democratic System Card can be applied and interpreted when using, for instance, representative or agonistic frames.

Moving on to additional related work, we expand on the related work described previously and describe how our framework relates to work on alignment, participatory AI, and human-centered AI.

Let's first consider alignment. Alignment procedures, such as reinforcement learning from human feedback or RLHF, and Constitutional AI generally aim to make AI systems more aligned with the preferences, values, or goals of humans. However, democratic considerations are often not central to these methods. For example, the annotators recruited for preference learning methods like RLHF or direct preference optimization, often have a highly unrepresentative sampling of the user population. Moreover, standard versions of these methods assume that collected preference comparisons come from one human, even when they actually come from many annotators who may have differing preferences.

Recently, there have been growing efforts to make alignment methods more democratic. For example, Anthropic's Collective Constitutional AI uses Pol.is to identify principles that an AI should follow from a representative sample of U.S. adults. As another example, some researchers have called for explicitly applying social choice approaches to preference learning methods, instead of implicitly interpreting different annotators as one person. While these nascent efforts are exciting, there is, as of yet, no standard way to evaluate how democratic these new alignment approaches are. Our framework aims to provide such guidelines.

Now, let's consider participatory AI. Another emerging approach that offers engagement with diverse voices in the design and deployment of AI is participatory AI. This encompasses a range of processes to engage people, from approaches that consult with stakeholders, for example, expressing preferences for policies in a ranking, through to those in which stakeholders own the design process and play a central role in shaping the procedures for deliberation as well as deciding on the outcomes of the process. Although this space shows promise, progress is nascent. In the private domain, efforts to date have largely been confined to consultation. In the public sphere, the implementation of participatory approaches faces difficulties in integrating with existing institutional rules and frameworks and agreeing on who should participate. Overall, it is felt by some that further clarity is required on the definition and role of participatory AI, and how it relates to other available approaches. We believe that the Democracy Levels Framework can help provide some of this clarity.

Finally, let’s discuss human-centered AI. Our Democracy Levels Framework strives to make AI systems more human-centered. By human-centered AI, we mean AI systems that are developed by drawing upon human-centered design methods, in addition to purely algorithmic ones, to ensure that the AI can better serve human needs. Democratic AI and traditional human-centered AI share many goals; advancing one can often advance the other. For example, democratic processes can involve iterative, deliberative decision-making from broad input, whereas traditional human-centered processes encourage iterative refinement through user and stakeholder feedback throughout the design lifecycle. Democratic processes empower individuals to collectively make governance and policy decisions that impact them, while human-centered design empowers users to shape systems in a way that aligns with their goals. As one moves up the Democracy Levels in our framework, constituents' agency is increasingly centered to provide them with more decision-making power over their AI systems.

Moving on to an example system card, we describe the Habermas Machine, an AI tool designed to support collective deliberation. The following is an example of a partial system card for this AI tool, as referenced in a work by Tessler in 2024.

In describing the context, the Habermas Machine represents a novel approach to collective deliberation. It leverages large language models, or LLMs, to facilitate the discovery of common ground among individuals with diverse perspectives. This system is inspired by Jürgen Habermas's theory of communicative action and employs LLMs to process personal opinions and critiques submitted by participants. Through iterative generation and refinement, the Habermas Machine produces group statements that aim to maximize collective endorsement and reflect shared understanding.

At its core, the system combines two fine-tuned LLMs: a generative model that proposes high-quality candidate group statements and a personalized reward model, abbreviated as PRM, that evaluates these candidates based on each participant’s personal opinion. The PRM outputs a personalized ranking of candidate statements for every participant. These rankings are then aggregated using a social choice function to select a group statement that aims to reflect broad agreement while incorporating critical minority viewpoints, facilitating inclusive and balanced consensus.

The Habermas Machine depends on and interacts with several other systems, which impact its success:

1. The quality of the initial opinions and critiques that participants provide is crucial. If users are not prepared to contribute in good faith or lack relevant information, the output of the Habermas Machine will be less effective.
2. The success of the Habermas Machine heavily relies on the capabilities of the underlying LLM to understand nuanced opinions, generate coherent and relevant group statements, and incorporate critiques effectively. Biases in the LLM could also impact the process.
3. A reliable and accessible platform is needed for participants to interact with the Habermas Machine, submit opinions and critiques, and receive group statements.
4. While AI-mediated, human oversight is still important to ensure the process is fair, address any unforeseen issues, and interpret the outputs in a real-world context.
5. The specific social or political issue being deliberated and the broader context of the deliberation can influence the effectiveness and impact of the Habermas Machine.
6. It's important to note that the Habermas Machine does not inform participants and is only focused on finding common ground among already informed participants. Separate systems have to be built to help inform participants.

Discussing process quality and representation, the system does not directly address the representation of participants in terms of demographic proportionality. Participants are grouped, but the system focuses on processing the content of their opinions, not ensuring a representative sample of the broader population is included in each group. The system aims to represent diverse viewpoints within the group by incorporating minority and majority opinions into the generated group statements.

However, the process does not directly ensure sufficient representation at critical parts of the process, such as proposing decisions or making ultimate decisions. There may also be barriers leading to bias in representation. As a technology-mediated system, the Habermas Machine may introduce potential barriers to entry related to technology. Lack of access to devices like computers, smartphones, tablets, and reliable internet connectivity could exclude certain segments of the population from participating, potentially leading to an underrepresentation of groups with lower digital literacy or limited technology access. Mitigations to this issue might include building voice access, apps, or providing supported access.

Moving to the informedness aspect, the Habermas Machine does not directly enhance participant informedness. Instead, it works with the opinions that participants already hold. The deliberation process itself, mediated by the AI, may indirectly increase informedness by exposing participants to diverse perspectives and prompting them to justify and critique opinions.

The Habermas Machine does not actively provide participants with additional context or information about trade-offs. However, participant informedness can increase as a result of participating in the deliberative process itself—engaging with AI-generated statements that synthesize different viewpoints. Rather than providing direct knowledge from experts, authorities, or stakeholders, this is a more indirect and emergent form of informedness. The system is designed to work with the existing knowledge and opinions of the participants, rather than to make them more informed in a traditional sense.

Focusing on deliberation, the Habermas Machine is explicitly designed to foster deliberation through an iterative process. Participants engage in multiple rounds of interaction: submitting initial opinions, reviewing and ranking AI-generated group statements, and providing critiques of the top-ranked statement. The AI mediator then uses these critiques to generate revised statements, continuing the cycle of refinement. This structured process encourages participants to consider diverse perspectives. However, it's still a very controlled form of deliberation, and no direct interaction between participants is possible.

This iterative nature, involving critique and revision rounds, encourages participants to move beyond initial, surface-level opinions. By requiring them to articulate critiques and respond to group statements, the process pushes them to consider underlying reasons and potentially evolve their perspectives towards a more nuanced understanding of the issue and shared values. While participants interact with the Habermas Machine individually, the system is designed to facilitate a collective deliberation. Participants are indirectly collaborating by contributing to a shared group statement. The Habermas Machine acts as a mediator to synthesize these individual contributions into a potentially consensual output.

Examining substantiveness, the Habermas Machine aims to produce substantive outputs through group statements that capture common ground. The iterative refinement process, incorporating critiques, moves beyond vague or simplistic statements towards more comprehensive and nuanced representations of the group's collective perspective on a complex issue.

While the group statements produced by the Habermas Machine are in natural language and can be relatively detailed, they are not formal policy documents and are not directly actionable and implementable. However, group statements are intended to be clear and informative around issues, enhancing quality. This improved quality results from using fine-tuned LLMs. Designed for complex issues, the Habermas Machine's iteration and diverse inputs allow statements to reflect multiple facets and nuances. Full complexity capture depends on input quality and LLM synthesis. However, the absence of direct interaction, such as caucus mediation, might reduce complexity. The system implicitly acknowledges uncertainty by surfacing diverse views and allowing for critiques. In its current form, the Habermas Machine generates a single output. A system that represents a distribution over outputs might be better at managing uncertainty. Since the outputs are not intended to be directly implementable, the process does not have additional measures to account for risk in implementability.

Considering robustness, the process's robustness is not fully tested. However, iteration and critique may offer some resilience. The iterative critique could mitigate adversarial behavior, as statements can be challenged and refined. Still, someone posing a very extreme opinion might be able to influence the model more than others.

The Habermas Machine’s effectiveness relies on the sincere input of its users, making it vulnerable to strategic manipulation if participants misrepresent their opinions to skew the outcome. The system is susceptible to coordinated biased input and limited safeguards against attacks. Transparency could offer some defense against false claims.

Focusing on legibility, the Habermas Machine's process is moderately legible. The steps of opinion submission, statement generation, critique, and revision are conceptually straightforward. The final output, a group statement in natural language, is understandable. However, the inner workings of the LLM and the precise algorithms used to generate and select statements are less transparent to non-participants.

The information regarding processes and systems used to make decisions is less transparent concerning the reasoning behind the specific wording of the group statement. While intermediate statements and personalized rankings allow for inspection, the specific AI algorithms and weighting of opinions are not easily auditable by non-participants. Verifying the robustness of the Habermas Machine would require technical expertise to evaluate the AI models and experimental methodology. For non-participants, robustness is largely a matter of trust in the research and the described process rather than direct verification. The group statement, as the "decision," is presented in a clear, natural language format, making it understandable to non-participants. The final group statement represents a synthesis, not a breakdown of 'for' and 'against' positions. While the Habermas Machine incorporates minority opinions, the output doesn't explicitly delineate which groups favored or opposed specific aspects of the issue.
