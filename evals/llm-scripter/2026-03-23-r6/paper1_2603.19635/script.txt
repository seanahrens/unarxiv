BEAVER: A Training-Free Hierarchical Prompt Compression Method via Structure-Aware Page Selection.

By Zhengpei Hu, Kai Li, Dapeng Fu, and 4 more authors.

Published on March 20, 2026.

The exponential expansion of context windows in large language models has unlocked capabilities for understanding long documents. However, it has also introduced severe bottlenecks in inference latency and information utilization. Existing compression methods often suffer from high training costs or semantic fragmentation due to aggressive token pruning. In this paper, the authors propose a novel training-free framework called "ours". 

This framework shifts compression from linear token removal to structure-aware hierarchical selection. "Ours" maximizes hardware parallelism by mapping variable-length contexts into dense page-level tensors using dual-path pooling. Additionally, it preserves discourse integrity through a hybrid planner that combines semantic and lexical dual-branch selection with sentence smoothing. 

The authors conducted extensive evaluations on four long-context benchmarks, demonstrating that "ours" achieves comparable performance to state-of-the-art methods like LongLLMLingua. Notably, on the RULER benchmark, "ours" maintains high fidelity in multi-needle retrieval where other baselines deteriorate. When it comes to efficiency, "ours" reduces latency by 26.4 times on 128 thousand contexts, providing a scalable solution for high-throughput applications. 

The authors have made their code available at "cslikai.cn/BEAVER".

In recent years, the context window size of large language models has expanded exponentially. It has evolved from the early 32 thousand token configuration to the million-token scale supported by models such as Claude 4.5 and Gemini 3.0. This advancement enables powerful capabilities for codebase analysis and global understanding across multiple long documents, while simultaneously revealing several critical bottlenecks that hinder practical deployment.

The first challenge is known as the "computation wall" in the inference phase. While quantization and system-level optimizations help to reduce memory pressure, the order L squared complexity of self-attention still causes prefill latency to surge with increasing context length. This leads to excessive time to first token and tail latency.

The second challenge is the "diminishing returns" in information utilization. Extending context windows does not yield proportional gains and often triggers what is referred to as the "Lost in the Middle" effect, where models overlook key information. These challenges underscore that simply scaling the length of the context cannot ensure robustness, necessitating a new paradigm that balances efficiency and accuracy.

To address these challenges, prompt compression offers an efficient solution by pruning redundancy while preserving semantics. Existing approaches fall into two paradigms: The first is unsupervised statistical methods, such as Selective-Context and LongLLMLingua, which filter tokens based on perplexity or self-information from off-the-shelf models. The second is supervised and specialized learning methods, like CPC, LLMLingua, and LLMLingua-2, which treat compression as a classification or ranking task using trained encoders to achieve higher precision.

However, there are two limitations that hinder practical deployment. First, reliance on training incurs significant overhead and limits cross-model generalization, undermining the goal of lightweight inference. Second, unstructured token pruning disrupts semantic and syntactic coherence, resulting in fragmented contexts that impede long-sequence modeling.

To address these limitations, we propose a novel training-free framework called \ours. This framework advances from linear token-wise pruning to structure-aware hierarchical selection. It comprises three key components: a Segmenter that converts sequences into 2D page tensors to optimize GPU efficiency and preserve discourse; a PageEncoder that employs dual-path pooling to capture hierarchical features; and a QueryPlanner that integrates a triple structural prior—anchors, flow, and flash—to mimic human cognition and suppress semantic drift. Finally, a smoothing mechanism restores selections into coherent, high-fidelity compressed text.

Extensive evaluations on four long-context benchmarks, namely LongBench, ZeroSCROLLS, RULER, and L-Eval, demonstrate that \ours achieves performance on par with, or superior to, state-of-the-art methods like LongLLMLingua and LLMLingua-2. Notably, \ours dominates the challenging RULER benchmark, nearly doubling the performance of existing baselines. In terms of efficiency, it yields a 26.4 times speedup over LongLLMLingua at 128 thousand context. Moreover, as a training-free framework, \ours proves robust across diverse model scales ranging from 0.6 billion to 32 billion parameters, positioning it as a scalable solution for high-throughput long-document understanding.

Moving on to the related work section. With the expansion of context windows in large language models, prompt compression has become a vital strategy to mitigate inference costs by pruning redundant information while preserving core semantics. Current research in hard prompt compression, which maintains discrete tokens for interpretability and compatibility, can be categorized into two primary paradigms.

The first paradigm consists of unsupervised statistical methods that rely on the perplexity or generation probabilities of off-the-shelf models without specialized training. For instance, Selective-Context and LongLLMLingua utilize self-information metrics to identify and prune redundant tokens or chunks. While efficient, these methods are often limited by their reliance on heuristic information-theoretic metrics.

The second paradigm involves supervised and specialized learning methods, which optimize model weights specifically for compression objectives. LLMLingua and LLMLingua-2 reformulate compression as a token-level classification task via data distillation, while PCRL and TACO-RL employ reinforcement learning for task-aware filtering. Additionally, works like CPC, AdaComp, and TCRA-LLM leverage specialized semantic encoders or embeddings to perform relevance filtering at the sentence or paragraph level, thereby mitigating local semantic fragmentation.

However, these methods face a dichotomy: learning-based approaches suffer from high deployment costs and limited transferability, while fine-grained token pruning disrupts syntactic coherence. Distinct from these methods, a new approach introduces a training-free, structure-aware framework. By shifting from token-wise to segment-page selection, it effectively eliminates training dependencies while preserving discourse integrity, addressing the fragmentation issues prevalent in prior methods.

Let's take a look at Figure 1, which illustrates the overall pipeline of this novel approach, named \ours. The pipeline comprises a Segmenter, a PageEncoder, and a QueryPlanner. This figure is a comprehensive flowchart showing these components in sequence, demonstrating how each part contributes to the process of context handling and prompt compression. The Segmenter is responsible for selecting relevant text segments, while the PageEncoder processes the entire page context, and the QueryPlanner orchestrates the interaction between the segments and the encoded context.

Moving on to the methods section.

In this section on the overall pipeline, the authors describe a method for processing a long-context sequence and a query. The long-context sequence is denoted as C, which is a one-dimensional array of length L sub c, representing the number of tokens. The query is represented by Q, which also is a one-dimensional array with a length of L sub q. The objective is to extract a compressed version of the context, which is represented by a tilde over C, with a much shorter length L sub p, while preserving the key information required to derive the correct answer A as much as possible.

As depicted in Figure 1, the framework named Ours consists of three key components: Segmenter, PageEncoder, and QueryPlanner. Specifically, Ours focuses on compressing only the portion of the context that precedes the query. If the query is explicit, the prefix before the query is compressed while retaining the query in full. If the query is implicit, it is defined as the final segment of the sequence. This segment is anchored to the nearest natural language boundary, using a backward window to prevent truncation.

The Segmenter then divides the context C into logical segments using semantic delimiters, such as newlines or heading markers, and proceeds with pagination to construct a structured two-dimensional page tensor P, represented in natural numbers with dimensions N by M. On this basis, the authors introduce a dual-path pooling encoder, called the PageEncoder, which is equipped with a context-statistics-adaptive weighting mechanism. This mechanism jointly encodes the paginated token tensor P to obtain page-level representations, denoted by a hat over P, in real numbers with dimensions N by d, where d indicates the feature dimension.

The QueryPlanner executes the central compression task by computing a hybrid semantic-lexical interaction score between the page-level representations and the query Q. It constructs a saliency mask by integrating structural priors, like anchor pages and flow pages, and selects high-scoring pages from the interaction score distribution. Subsequently, sentence-level smoothing is performed on the salient pages. Finally, the discrete mask is translated back into a continuous subsequence, denoted as a tilde over C, for downstream large language model inference.

The Segmenter is designed to efficiently map the sequence C, which has variable length, into a two-dimensional page matrix based on natural delimiters, denoted as D, such as newlines. The process begins by partitioning C into logical segments, which are represented as c sub 1 through c sub K. To construct the matrix, a greedy pagination strategy is employed with a capacity M. In this strategy, multiple consecutive segments are packed into a single page if their cumulative length is within the capacity M, thereby minimizing padding. If segments are longer, they are split across consecutive pages. The output of this process is a page index tensor P, which is padded with negative one. This enables efficient standard matrix multiplication while preserving local semantic boundaries.

The subsection introduces PageEncoder, a component used to enable QueryPlanner to efficiently perform coarse-grained selection. PageEncoder is a dual-path pooling encoder that captures both global semantics and salient local features. It does this through weighted average and max pooling, utilizing a context-adaptive weighting mechanism to mitigate semantic collapse caused by redundant tokens.

Specifically, given the large language model embedding denoted as E, which maps natural numbers to real numbers to the power of d, the token sequence is first mapped to token features. These features are represented as an L sub c by d matrix, where the feature of the l-th token is represented by h sub l, and it equals E of C sub l. Using the page index tensor represented by P generated by the Segmenter, the token features are rearranged into a page-level tensor X in the dimensions of N by M by d. Here, the feature at position i, j is defined as x sub i, j, which equals h sub l if P sub i, j equals l and is greater than or equal to zero. It equals zero if P sub i, j equals negative one.

To down-weight frequent but uninformative tokens, an in-context Inverse Term Frequency score is computed. Let the token frequency within the context be represented by tf of t. The weight is defined as: w superscript itf of t equals the normalization of the logarithm of one plus the sum of L sub c and L sub q divided by one plus tf of t. Here, the normalization function denotes min-max normalization to the range zero to one. The effective attention weight for position i, j is then omega sub i, j. This equals B sub i, j multiplied by w superscript itf of C sub P sub i, j, with a binary mask B sub i, j defined as the indicator function P sub i, j is greater than or equal to zero.

Next, complementary representations are extracted via two pooling paths. The weighted average pooling aggregates global semantics using the formula: mu sub i equals the sum from j equals one to M of omega sub i, j multiplied by x sub i, j, divided by the sum from j equals one to M of omega sub i, j plus epsilon, where epsilon ensures numerical stability. Simultaneously, the max pooling path captures salient local activations, such as rare keywords, by masking invalid positions with a large negative constant beta to ensure numerical correctness. The formula is m sub i equals the maximum where j ranges from one to M of B sub i, j multiplied by x sub i, j, plus one minus B sub i, j to be multiplied by beta. The final representation is a linear fusion of gamma multiplied by mu sub i, plus one minus gamma multiplied by m sub i, where gamma represents the fusion weights. All page representations are stacked into a matrix P-hat in the dimensions of N by d.

Similarly, for short queries with length less than four, a dense retrieval approach is adopted to obtain a unified semantic vector q in the real to the power of d space. For longer queries, where the length is greater than or equal to four, a late interaction strategy is employed. This retains fine-grained token representations to handle complex matching requirements.

Moving on to the Query Planner section, it identifies salient pages from the encoded set, denoted as P-hat, to construct a compressed context. To capture both semantic relevance and exact answer spans, it integrates dense retrieval and lexical overlap within a unified space, incorporating structural priors.

Given the query representation Q, which is a set of vectors q-sub-k for k equals one to K, where K is one for short queries, and the set of page vectors P-hat, which is a set of vectors p-hat sub-i for i equals one to N, a semantic score is computed using a weighted cosine similarity. To handle multi-vector queries effectively, the semantic score is defined as follows: s sub sem of i equals the sum over k from one to K of w sub q-sub-k superscript itf, multiplied by the dot product of p-hat sub-i and q-sub-k, divided by the product of the two norms. Here, w sub q-sub-k superscript itf is the ITF score of q-sub-k. This mechanism naturally assigns higher importance to discriminative terms, such as entities, over common stop words.

Complementarily, the lexical branch emphasizes exact token overlaps. Let T subscript Q represent the query token set and P subscript i represent the tokens in page i. We aggregate the importance of overlapping tokens using the following equation: s sub lex of i equals the sum over l in P sub i of the indicator function, which checks if C sub l is in T sub Q, multiplied by w sub C sub l superscript itf. Both scores are min-max normalized to a range from zero to one over all N pages. The final relevance score is a linear fusion given by s sub mix of i equals lambda times s sub sem of i plus one minus lambda times s sub lex of i, where lambda is a hyperparameter controlling the relative importance of semantic and lexical alignment. Tasks emphasizing semantic reasoning may assign a larger lambda, while those containing many code snippets or identifiers may benefit from a smaller lambda.

After computing the scores s sub mix of i, the Query Planner incorporates structural priors to enhance stability and discourse coherence. First, the query anchor, p subscript qry, is identified as the page containing the query's start, enforcing causal constraints where i is less than or equal to p subscript qry. The selection set comprises three distinct subsets: Anchors, Flow, and Flash. Anchors consist of the initial k sub anc pages preserved to retain global metadata such as the title and introductory definitions. Flow mimics human working memory by selecting a contiguous window immediately preceding the query, defined as indices i from the maximum of one or p subscript qry minus w sub flow to p subscript qry. Flash selects the top-k sub flash pages with the highest s sub mix of i to capture distant but critical evidence from remaining candidates.

Figure one illustrates sentence-level smoothing. To ensure syntactic completeness, the initially retrieved span represented by an orange solid box is extended outward to the nearest sentence boundaries, which are dashed boxes. Finally, the selected page indices are mapped back to token spans a sub r to b sub r. As illustrated, this smoothing starts at a sub r and ends at b sub r, extending to the nearest sentence boundaries to maintain syntactic completeness. Overlapping spans are merged, and the result is concatenated to form the final input C-tilde.

Moving on to the experiments section.

In the experimental setup, the authors comprehensively evaluate the effectiveness and robustness of their model, referred to as "\ours," by conducting experiments across four diverse and challenging long-context benchmarks.

First, they adopted "LongBench" and "ZeroSCROLLS." These benchmarks cover multiple tasks, including single-document and multi-document question answering, summarization, and few-shot learning. This approach provides a holistic view of general long-context capabilities.

Next, they used the high-quality synthetic benchmark known as "RULER" to test long-context understanding under varying context lengths. These lengths range from 16,000 to 128,000. The tasks include multi-needle retrieval and variable tracking, which are designed to precisely assess the model’s ability to retain fine-grained information.

Lastly, they evaluated the generalization performance of "\ours" using "L-Eval," which is specifically used for out-of-domain tasks. This benchmark enforces strict token-length constraints. More detailed descriptions of the datasets and the evaluation metrics can be found in the appendix under "Appendix: Datasets."

To ensure a fair comparison, they benchmarked "\ours" against several prompt compression methods. These baseline models are grouped into two categories. The first category consists of unsupervised statistical methods such as "Selective-Context" and "LongLLMLingua," which rely on intrinsic metrics like perplexity or self-information from off-the-shelf models to identify redundancy without the need for specialized training.

The second category includes supervised and specialized learning methods, which utilize models explicitly optimized for compression tasks or leverage specialized semantic encoders. Examples of these are "LLMLingua" and "LLMLingua-2." Additionally, they included embedding-based retrieval approaches such as "SBERT" and "OpenAI Embeddings," which employ trained encoders to perform semantic relevance filtering. Further details on baseline configurations and implementations can be located in the appendix under "Appendix: Baselines."

For the implementation details, the authors followed existing methods and uniformly adopted "gpt-3.5-turbo-instruct" as the large language model for all downstream task evaluations. The model can be explored at platform.openai.com/docs/models/gpt-3.5-turbo-instruct. The PageEncoder used embeddings from "Qwen3-8B," which can be found at huggingface.co/Qwen/Qwen3-8B.

Regarding the hyperparameters, they set the page size to 64, the fusion weight to 0.7, and the scoring parameter also to 0.7. The structural priors were fixed with the anchor and flow weights both set at 4, and the flash set size was dynamically adapted to fully utilize the remaining token budget.

Their evaluations strictly adhered to benchmark-specific context budgets and used official standard prompt templates to ensure fair comparisons. Lastly, all latency and throughput metrics were measured on NVIDIA A100 80 gigabyte GPUs.

In this paper, we present a new framework, referred to as Ours, which is a hardware-efficient and training-free prompt compression framework. It is specifically designed to overcome the computational and memory bottlenecks associated with long-context large language models. By introducing a hierarchical segment-page mechanism, we are able to transform irregular sequence compression into optimized tensor operations. 

Our approach leverages unsupervised in-context inverse term frequency weighting and a structure-aware hybrid planner. This effectively retains both global semantics and fine-grained lexical details, thereby avoiding the semantic collapse that is common in probability-based pruning methods. 

Experimental results confirm that Ours establishes state-of-the-art performance in question-answering and fine-grained retrieval tasks, while maintaining highly competitive fidelity in summarization tasks. Crucially, it offers superior scalability, achieving a twenty-six point four times speedup on 128 thousand contexts when compared to learning-based baselines. 

As a plug-and-play module that requires no parameter updates, Ours provides a practical and robust foundation for efficient large-scale long-document understanding.

There are several limitations associated with \ours. Although it achieves a favorable balance between efficiency and performance, some constraints must be acknowledged. First, the use of page-level granularity prioritizes structural integrity and speed. However, it is inherently less precise than fine-grained token pruning, which may sometimes result in the retention of minor redundancies within selected segments. Second, the retrieval mechanism in \ours relies on semantic and lexical similarity. This approach might face challenges in deep multi-hop reasoning scenarios. In such cases, the supporting evidence shares little direct overlap with the query and requires intermediate deductive steps. Finally, as \ours is a training-free method, it depends on pre-set hyperparameters, such as weighting factors, that might require manual adjustment to achieve optimal results across drastically different domains. This is unlike end-to-end learning models that adapt automatically.

Ethical considerations are an important aspect of this research work, which introduces a prompt compression framework named \ours. This framework is designed to improve the efficiency of Long Context LLMs. While the method aims to reduce computational costs, there are potential risks associated with information pruning.

The primary risk of any compression method, including \ours, is the potential loss of critical qualifiers or safety constraints. For example, negations or warnings present in the original context might be lost. In sensitive domains such as legal or medical analysis, which were evaluated in the authors' experiments on L-Eval, such omissions could lead to factually incorrect or unsafe downstream generations. Although the structure-aware design and sentence smoothing aim to mitigate fragmentation, users should exercise caution and verify outputs when applying this method to high-stakes decision-making tasks.

The framework \ours relies on off-the-shelf embedding models, such as Qwen, to calculate page importance. Biases that are inherent in these pre-trained models may be propagated. For example, the under-representation of certain dialects or cultural contexts could result in the systematic filtering of text from minority groups if the embedding model deems such patterns as "low importance" or "redundant". Future work should investigate the fairness of relevance scoring across diverse linguistic demographics.

In this section on unsupervised statistical methods, we begin with a technique proposed by Li and colleagues in 2023, called Selective-Context. This method introduces a context compression technique aimed at enhancing the efficiency of inference in large language models by identifying and pruning redundant input information. The method utilizes a base causal language model to evaluate the self-information of lexical units, such as sentences or tokens. This process involves selectively retaining content that has higher information values while discarding predictable redundancy.

Next, we have the work of Jiang and colleagues in 2024, titled LongLLMLingua. This approach addresses the challenges of high computational cost, noise, and position bias that are inherent in long-context scenarios. Building upon their previous work, LLMLingua, the method introduces a question-aware coarse-to-fine compression approach. This aims to enhance the density of key information relative to the user's query. Furthermore, the method incorporates a document reordering strategy to mitigate the "lost-in-the-middle" phenomenon. It also employs a post-compression subsequence recovery mechanism to preserve the integrity of critical details.

Moving on to supervised and specialized learning methods, we first consider LLMLingua, as discussed in the work by Jiang and colleagues in 2023. This approach introduces a coarse-to-fine prompt compression framework aimed at accelerating inference by pruning prompts without losing their semantic integrity. The method employs a budget controller to dynamically allocate compression ratios across different prompt components. It utilizes a token-level iterative algorithm to capture conditional dependencies between tokens. Additionally, LLMLingua incorporates instruction tuning to align the distribution of the smaller compression model with the target large language model.

Next, Sentence-BERT, detailed in the research by Reimers and Gurevych in 2019, modifies the pretrained BERT architecture by employing siamese and triplet network structures. This setup enables the framework to derive semantically meaningful sentence embeddings. It allows for sentence comparisons using cosine similarity, which significantly reduces the computational overhead associated with pair-wise cross-encoding while maintaining high performance on semantic search and clustering tasks.

Finally, LLMLingua-2, as explained by Pan and associates in 2024, advances task-agnostic prompt compression by framing the problem as token classification instead of relying on causal entropy-based pruning. The authors distilled knowledge from GPT-4 to create a high-quality extractive compression dataset. This dataset is then utilized to train a bidirectional Transformer encoder, such as XLM-RoBERTa or mBERT, to classify tokens as "preserve" or "discard". This approach enables the model to take full advantage of bidirectional context for better information retention, thereby ensuring faithfulness to the original text while achieving significantly lower latency compared to autoregressive or causal-based methods.

Moving on to the qualitative comparison with baseline methods, the authors focus on providing an intuitive assessment of their method under strict inference constraints. They conducted qualitative analyses on four representative tasks from the L-Eval benchmark. These tasks include code generation, referred to as CodeU; government report summarization, identified as GovReport; financial question answering, referred to as LongFQA; and few shot mathematical reasoning, called GSM100. A strict length budget ranging from 500 to 1500 tokens was imposed to evaluate each method's ability to preserve critical information under extreme compression. The authors compared their method with unsupervised statistical methods such as LongLLMLingua, and supervised methods such as LLMLingua 2. The focus was on failure modes related to semantic noise, program logic, chain of thought structure, and code syntax.

Firstly, in the LongFQA task, the model faced substantial semantic noise under a 500 token budget. The goal was to extract the pricing logic of the iPhone 15 Pro from a context mixed with irrelevant inventory information about consumer electronics like NVIDIA GPUs and Dyson vacuum cleaners, as depicted in Figure 1. The findings reveal that the statistical baseline, labeled as Baseline A, excessively compresses information and corrupts critical surface forms. For instance, it transforms the key surcharge term "20 percent" into "a2 percent," breaking the numerical logic. Meanwhile, the supervised baseline, Baseline B, although producing fluent text, fails to preserve the causal relation between the base price and the surcharge rule, leading to hallucination and an incorrect output of 899 dollars. In contrast, the authors' method uses a hybrid semantic and lexical scoring mechanism to identify and retain the price table and the surcharge memo scattered across the document, while filtering the intervening noise blocks. This approach enables the downstream model to correctly infer the final price of $1,078.80, demonstrating strong robustness to distraction.

Next, for the GovReport summarization task under a 500 token budget, which tests long document coherence and logical completeness, the goal is to extract the procedural logic of the "72 hour rule" and its exemption conditions, as shown in Figure 2. Baselines exhibit severe semantic fragmentation. The statistical method damages the syntactic structure, generating corrupted fragments such as "Rements R", which impedes rule extraction. The supervised method retains keywords like "one day layover" but loses the connecting logic that triggers the exemption, notably "convening two legislative days", resulting in a factually incomplete summary. By contrast, the authors' method leverages a Segmenter to respect natural language boundaries, preserving complete sentence structure for both the rule and its exceptions, and yields a coherent and accurate summary.

Thirdly, in the GSM100 few shot reasoning task with a 1500 token budget, the authors evaluate whether compression preserves the chain of thought structure, as illustrated in Figure 3. Baselines often treat few shot demonstrations as unstructured text, disrupting the boundaries between question, reasoning, and answer. This causes the large model to miss the in-context examples, degrading to zero shot guessing which fails on nontrivial arithmetic. The authors' method treats each example as a coherent page unit and preserves the chain of thought steps from highly relevant demonstrations, such as "Let’s think step by step...". This approach enables genuine in-context learning and guides the model to the correct result, two plus one equals three.

Finally, in the CodeU code task under a 1000 token budget, where syntactic correctness is critical, baselines struggle more severely, as depicted in Figure 4. The statistical method indiscriminately removes tokens deemed redundant, including structural delimiters, resulting in unparsable code such as “importLib np..”. The supervised method may collapse, compressing complex logic into meaningless punctuation. Due to the high information density of code, the authors' method prioritizes rare tokens and structural separators through the lexical pathway in PageEncoder, preserving function definitions and the demonstration examples in docstrings. This approach allows the model to pass syntax checks and learn from examples to correctly predict execution outcomes.

In this section, the authors provide a detailed analysis of the scalability results, focusing on the performance of their method, referred to as "ours," across the Qwen3 series, which ranges from 0.6 billion to 32 billion. This analysis is presented in Table One. The performance of "ours" is compared to two categories of baselines, which are defined in the related work. These categories are Unsupervised Statistical Methods and Supervised Specialized Learning Methods.

In the subsection titled "Analysis versus Unsupervised Statistical Methods," the authors discuss their findings on unsupervised methods like LongLLMLingua, which use causal entropy or perplexity metrics to identify key information. These metrics are generally effective for larger models. However, the authors' experiments reveal a fragility when applied to lightweight architectures.

They reference Table 1 to illustrate that LongLLMLingua achieves an average score of only 25.9 on Qwen3-0.6B. This performance drop shows that probability distributions in smaller models are significantly noisier and less reliable as proxies for semantic importance compared to larger models.

Additionally, LongLLMLingua's "question-aware" mechanism relies on the model's zero-shot understanding of the query. This understanding is inherently limited in 0.6B-scale models. Contrarily, the authors' approach makes use of intrinsic attention statistics, achieving a score of, notably, 76.8 on the same 0.6B model. This substantial difference demonstrates that attention-based signals are much more robust and invariant to model scale than the perplexity-based signals employed by other unsupervised baseline methods.

In the comparison of analysis versus supervised and specialized learning methods, the authors discuss challenges faced by supervised methods such as LLMLingua and LLMLingua-2. For these methods, the main issue with smaller models is the distribution mismatch between the compression module and the target inference model. Taking LLMLingua-2 as an example, it uses a BERT-based encoder that is trained on data distilled from GPT-4. While this setup is effective in capturing information relevant to GPT-4, the patterns it identifies do not transfer well to the Qwen3-0.6B model, which scores 32.9. 

The problem arises because the tokens considered "informative" by a compressor aligned with GPT-4 are often too complex or distinct from the reasoning paths required by a more lightweight model. Furthermore, LLMLingua's instruction tuning strategy does not effectively align when the target model lacks sufficient capacity, leading to the lowest recorded score of 25.1.

In contrast, the approach referred to as \ours in the text, avoids these compatibility issues by design. \ours derives compression decisions directly from the specific model instance being utilized. This ensures that the retained information is inherently aligned with that particular model's processing mechanisms. This "self-adaptive" characteristic of \ours allows it to maintain a 98 percent performance retention on the 0.6B model without the necessity for external training or domain adaptation.
